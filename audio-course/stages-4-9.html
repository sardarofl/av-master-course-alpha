Zone Distribution

Mixer → Distribution Amp → Multiple Zones

One to Many Audio Distribution

What if you need to send the same audio to multiple rooms, floors, or areas? A hotel lobby, restaurant, and hallways all playing background music? This is where zone distribution systems come in.

The Signal Flow

What is a Distribution Amplifier?

🔊 Distribution Amp Function

A distribution amplifier (or "zone amplifier") takes one audio input and creates multiple amplified outputs, each with independent volume control.

  • Input: Line-level stereo signal from mixer
  • Outputs: Multiple speaker-level zones (typically 4-16 zones)
  • Per-zone control: Volume, on/off, sometimes EQ
  • Power: Each zone typically 15-100W

🏢 Use Cases

  • Hotels: Lobby, restaurant, gym, pool, hallways
  • Retail: Sales floor, fitting rooms, stockroom
  • Offices: Reception, conference rooms, break rooms
  • Restaurants: Dining room, bar, patio, bathrooms
  • Schools: Classrooms, gym, cafeteria, halls

70V/100V Constant Voltage Systems

Why 70V Systems?

For commercial installations with many speakers over long distances, we use constant voltage distribution (70V in North America, 100V in Europe/Asia).

❌ Traditional Low-Impedance (8Ω)

  • Limited number of speakers per amp
  • Complex impedance calculations
  • Thick cables needed for long runs
  • Power loss over distance

✅ 70V Constant Voltage

  • Connect dozens of speakers easily
  • Simple transformer-based taps
  • Thin cables work for long distances
  • Minimal power loss

How 70V Systems Work

1

Amplifier Steps Up Voltage

The distribution amp outputs 70V AC signal (instead of typical speaker-level voltage)

2

Speakers Have Transformers

Each speaker has a built-in transformer that steps the voltage back down and "taps" a specific wattage (1W, 2W, 4W, 8W, etc.)

3

Power Budget

Add up all speaker taps. Total must not exceed amp power rating.

Example: 100W amp can drive 50× 2W speakers, or 25× 4W speakers

70V Wiring Topology

Speakers connect in parallel. Total: 2W + 4W + 2W + 8W = 16W (well under typical 100W amp capacity)

Zone Control & Features

Independent Volume Control

Each zone has its own volume knob or digital control. The restaurant can be louder than the lobby, hallways quieter at night.

Zone Override

Priority paging systems can override music in specific zones for announcements (e.g., "Security to the front desk").

Scheduling

Modern systems can schedule zones: gym music on 6am-10pm, restaurant quiet during off-hours, lobby 24/7.

Source Selection

Advanced systems let different zones play different sources: lobby plays corporate radio, restaurant plays Spotify, gym plays streaming service.

Real-World Scenarios

🏨 Boutique Hotel

System: 6-zone distribution amplifier (350W total)

Zones:

  • Lobby: 4× 8W ceiling speakers = 32W
  • Restaurant: 8× 4W ceiling speakers = 32W
  • Bar: 4× 8W wall speakers = 32W
  • Pool area: 6× 8W outdoor speakers = 48W
  • Gym: 4× 4W ceiling speakers = 16W
  • Hallways (3 floors): 20× 2W ceiling speakers = 40W

Total: 200W (leaves 150W headroom)

Control: Wall-mounted zone controllers in each area

🛍️ Retail Store Chain

System: 4-zone amp per store

Zones:

  • Sales floor: 12× 4W ceiling speakers
  • Fitting rooms: 4× 2W in-ceiling speakers
  • Stockroom: 3× 4W utility speakers
  • Break room: 2× 4W speakers

Source: Corporate streaming service with branded playlists

Feature: Paging microphone overrides music for staff announcements

Advantages vs. Limitations

✅ Advantages of Zone Systems

  • Cost-effective: One source, one amp, many speakers
  • Centralized control: Change music/volume from one location
  • Simple wiring: Especially with 70V systems
  • Scalable: Add zones or speakers easily
  • Reliable: Fewer components to fail

⚠️ Limitations

  • Same content: All zones typically play the same source (unless using matrix switcher - see Stage 5!)
  • Audio quality: 70V transformers can color sound slightly
  • Not for critical listening: Fine for background music, but not studio-quality
  • No individual speaker control: Volume is per-zone, not per-speaker

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Zone distribution sends one audio source to multiple areas with independent volume control
  • Distribution amplifiers take line-level input and create multiple speaker-level zone outputs
  • 70V/100V systems use constant voltage for commercial installations with many speakers over long distances
  • Speaker taps (1W, 2W, 4W, etc.) determine power draw; total taps must not exceed amp capacity
  • Perfect for: Hotels, retail, restaurants, offices, schools - anywhere background music/paging is needed
  • Each zone gets independent volume control, but usually plays the same source (next stage adds source routing!)
