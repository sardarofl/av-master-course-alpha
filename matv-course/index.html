RF & Signal Fundamentals

Every MATV and IPTV system starts with RF signal. Understanding levels, noise, frequency and quality metrics is the foundation that separates guesswork from professional design.

Signal Level: dBμV vs dBmV vs dBm

Television distribution uses dBμV (decibels relative to 1 microvolt across 75Ω) as the standard unit. Know the conversions cold — surveyors and headend engineers use all three.

RF Frequency Spectrum — TV Bands

Simplified TV frequency allocation — UHF is the primary terrestrial TV band; SAT IF is the downconverted satellite signal carried on coax

Typical Signal Levels (IEC/European)

  • Antenna output: 45–80 dBμV
  • Headend input: 55–80 dBμV
  • Headend output: 100–114 dBμV
  • Wall outlet (terminal): 47–77 dBμV
  • STB / TV input: 45–77 dBμV
  • Minimum usable: 45 dBμV (analogue: 57 dBμV)

Signal Impairments

  • Noise Floor: thermal noise; keeps SNR usable
  • Reflections / Echoes: ghosting / multipath
  • Intermodulation: mixing of carriers → IM3 products
  • Compression: amplifier driven beyond 1dB C/P
  • Ingress: interference from LTE/mobile into cable plant
  • Tilt: higher frequencies attenuate more in coax
Digital Quality Metrics: MER & BER

Analogue TV uses S/N ratio. Digital TV lives and dies by MER and BER — signal is perfect until it's not.

MER (Modulation Error Ratio)
Equivalent to SNR for digital modulation — measures how far constellation points deviate from ideal. Higher = better. Minimum for reliable QAM-64: ≥26 dB; QAM-256: ≥32 dB.
BER (Bit Error Ratio)
Raw errors before FEC (pre-Viterbi) and after FEC (post-Viterbi). Target: post-Viterbi BER < 1×10⁻⁶. A "cliff effect" occurs — below threshold, picture freezes/breaks up suddenly.
Noise Margin (Cliff)
Digital has no graceful degradation. A signal 1 dB above threshold = perfect picture. 1 dB below = no picture. Always design with ≥3–6 dB safety margin.
C/N (Carrier to Noise)
Used in satellite calculations. DVB-S2 with 8PSK requires C/N ≥ 9 dB; with QPSK (lowest FEC): ≥ 3 dB. Must account for rain fade margin.
⚠ Cliff Effect — Common Site Mistake

Installers testing digital signal at 50 dBμV see a perfect picture and assume the system is fine. Rain fade or cable aging later drops the signal 4 dB — the picture simply vanishes. Always measure MER, not just level, and design to IEC 60728-11 minimum margins.

Impedance & Reflections (VSWR / Return Loss)

Why 75 Ω?

TV distribution uses 75 Ω coax — a compromise between lowest loss (~77 Ω) and highest power (~30 Ω). All connectors, splitters, taps, amps, and outlets must be 75 Ω. Mixing impedances causes reflections.

Return Loss

Measures how well a device is matched. Return Loss (dB) = −20 log|Γ|. A good splitter: >20 dB return loss. Poor terminations (<10 dB) cause standing waves and picture ripple. Always terminate unused ports with 75 Ω caps.

Return Loss ↔ Reflection Coefficient
Return Loss (dB) = −20 × log₁₀(|Γ|)
VSWR = (1 + |Γ|) / (1 − |Γ|)
20 dB Return Loss → |Γ| = 0.1 → VSWR = 1.22:1 — acceptable

The headend is the brain of a MATV system — it receives broadcast signals, processes them, and outputs a clean composite signal ready for distribution. This stage covers everything from rooftop aerials to channel processors.

Pro Tip — Antenna Array Combining

Combining two identical aerials in phase adds +3 dB gain. Stack vertically (½λ spacing) for horizontal gain; mount side-by-side for improved F/B ratio. Useful in fringe areas without individual high-gain antenna pointing issues.

Mast Head Amplifiers (MHAs)

Why MHA Before Cable Run?

Coax adds noise (attenuation = noise figure). A 10 dB cable loss adds 10 dB to system noise figure. By amplifying first at the mast, the cable loss no longer dominates overall noise performance — Friis' noise formula in practice.

Key MHA Specifications

  • Gain: 15–25 dB typical
  • Noise Figure (NF): <2 dB (best: 0.5–1 dB)
  • IP3 (intermodulation): >50 dBm output IP3
  • AGC (automatic gain control) — required in strong-signal areas
  • DC power: supplied via coax from indoor unit (5–24V)
⚠ Strong Signal Overload — Common Mistake

In city centres with strong transmitters, MHAs can overload and create intermodulation distortion. Use an attenuator or switch to a distribution amplifier with lower gain and high IP3. Always check output level against amplifier's maximum output rating (typically 110–120 dBμV).

Channel Processors & Transmodulators
Channel Processor
Receives a single RF channel, filters it, amplifies it, and re-outputs it on the same or a different channel number. Essential for cleaning up weak signals and avoiding adjacent channel interference.
Transmodulator (IRD)
Integrated Receiver-Decoder: receives satellite DVB-S/S2 signal, decodes a multiplex (or CAM-decrypts), then re-encodes to DVB-T or DVB-C for distribution. Converts satellite content to format STBs/TVs can tune natively.
Signal Strip Amplifier
Strips the pilot carrier, amplifies, and re-injects. Used to boost individual channels without an IRD. Lower cost but no decryption capability.
Frequency Conversion
Move channels to avoid clashes (e.g., move sat-derived HD channel from Ch 21 to Ch 55). Essential in mixed headends where terrestrial and satellite channels overlap.

Universal LNB (Standard)

Dual local oscillator: 9.75 GHz (low band) and 10.6 GHz (high band) switched by 22 kHz tone. Single output. Used with one receiver. Polarisation: V=13V, H=18V from receiver.

Quattro LNB (Multiswitch Ready)

4 fixed outputs: Low-H, Low-V, High-H, High-V — each a dedicated port. Designed exclusively for multiswitches. Does NOT work direct to a receiver (no 22kHz switching needed).

DiSEqC, Unicable & Multiswitches
DiSEqC 1.0
4-switch control (A/B/C/D) via superimposed 22 kHz burst tone on coax. Commands: Committed (LNB select) + Uncommitted (multiswitch bank). Industry standard for multi-dish, multi-LNB systems.
DiSEqC 1.2 / USALS
Motor control for single-dish multi-satellite systems. USALS (Universal Satellites Automatic Location System) calculates motor angle from GPS coordinates — set once, auto-finds all satellites.
Unicable (EN 50494)
Single-cable multiswitch standard — up to 8 user bands (Unicable I) or 32 bands (Unicable II / EN 50607) on one coax. Each STB tunes to a unique frequency slot. Eliminates multi-cable wiring — critical for MDU/hotels.
Multiswitch
Matrix switch connecting Quattro LNB outputs (4 lines) to multiple subscriber outputs (8–32+). Each output provides any polarisation/band combination on demand. Cascadeable for large installations.

Quattro LNB → 9-input multiswitch (4 sat + 4 terrestrial + 1 spare) → 16 subscriber coax drops, each capable of any sat tuning combination

Rain Fade & Link Budget
Simplified Link Budget (Ku-Band Downlink)
EIRP (dBW) − Free Space Path Loss − Atmospheric Loss + G/T (dB/K) − Receiver NF = C/N (dB)
Free space loss @ 12 GHz, 36,000 km ≈ 205.6 dB. G/T depends on dish diameter and LNB noise temperature.
⚠ Rain Fade Margin — Critical for Hotels

Ku-band suffers rain attenuation — up to 10 dB/km at heavy rain in tropics. A marginally designed system works in sun but loses signal in storms. Always design with ≥3 dB rain fade margin for UK, ≥6 dB for Middle East/tropics. Larger dish = more margin.

Pro Tip — Cable Tilt

Coax attenuates more at high frequencies. A 100m CT-100 run loses ~9.8 dB at 800 MHz but only ~3.2 dB at 100 MHz. This "tilt" compounds through multiple cables. Equalising amplifiers and slope-compensating taps correct this to keep all channels level at outlets.

Passive Components — Splitters, Taps & Couplers

Key passive distribution components and their insertion loss characteristics — tap value selection drives the entire signal budget

Signal Level Budget — Design Methodology

A signal budget traces the signal from headend output to each outlet, summing all gains and losses to ensure every outlet falls within the target window (47–77 dBμV in Europe).

Signal Budget (per outlet)
Level at outlet (dBμV) = Headend output − Σ(cable loss) − Σ(splitter/tap loss) + Σ(amplifier gain)
Example: 110 dBμV headend − 15 dB cable − 7.5 dB (4-way split) − 4 dB tap − 3 dB outlet = 80.5 dBμV ✓

Tap Selection Strategy

  • Near headend: high-value tap (e.g., 20 dB) — large signal, small tap out
  • Mid-run: medium tap (e.g., 12 dB)
  • End of run: low-value tap (e.g., 4 dB) — signal weak, need to use most of it
  • Terminate final port with 75 Ω dummy load

Distribution Amplifiers

  • Used when cable run exceeds budget
  • Gain: 25–35 dB; Max output: 110–120 dBμV
  • Every amp adds noise and distortion — minimise cascade depth
  • Max cascade: 3–4 amps before noise/IM floor rises unacceptably
  • Use slope-compensating amps to correct cable tilt
⚠ Amplifier Cascade — Noise & Distortion Accumulation

Each amplifier in cascade adds noise and intermodulation products. With 4 amplifiers in cascade, noise figure increases by 6 dB and CTB/CSO distortion products accumulate. Never cascade more amps than necessary — redesign the passive network first.

MPEG-2 Transport Stream (TS) Anatomy

All DVB standards carry an MPEG-2 Transport Stream — a 188-byte packet-multiplexed bitstream. Understanding its structure is essential for IPTV headend engineers who re-mux or transcode content.

Every DVB transmission is a multiplex of 188-byte TS packets from multiple services — PIDs route each component to the right decoder

PSI/SI Tables — Service Discovery
PAT (PID 0x00)
Program Association Table — lists all services in the multiplex with their PMT PID. The receiver reads PAT first to find all programs.
PMT (per service)
Program Map Table — for each service, lists the PID of each component (video, audio, subtitles, teletext, PCR). Decoder uses this to assemble the program.
NIT (PID 0x10)
Network Information Table — describes the physical delivery system (frequencies, symbol rates, modulation, network name). Used for automatic channel search (NIT scan).
SDT (PID 0x11)
Service Description Table — service names, provider names, service type (TV/radio/data). Populates the channel list display on the TV.
EIT (PID 0x12)
Event Information Table — EPG data (programme name, description, start time, duration, content rating). EIT actual (current transponder) + EIT other (other transponders).
⚠ SI Table Errors Break Channel Lists

When headend transmodulators re-mux channels, they must regenerate or correctly pass-through PSI/SI tables. Incorrect Network IDs, scrambled PMTs, or missing SDTs cause TVs to fail channel searches or display blank channel names — a common commissioning fault.

Conditional Access (CA) & Scrambling

DVB-CSA Scrambling

DVB Common Scrambling Algorithm encrypts the payload at the TS packet level. Control Words (CWs) change every ~10 seconds. CWs are delivered in Entitlement Control Messages (ECMs) within the TS, encrypted with subscriber-specific keys.

CAM (Conditional Access Module)

CI (Common Interface) slot + CAM allows a generic TV or STB to decrypt any CA system. The CAM contains the CA-specific smartcard. Headend IRDs use embedded CAMs to decrypt subscription content for free-to-air redistribution within licensed areas.

Stage 6 of 16

IPTV Architecture Overview

IPTV delivers television over IP networks instead of RF coax. This architectural shift — from broadcast to unicast/multicast — changes everything: how headends work, how networks must be designed, and how viewers receive content.

IPTV Service Types

Live TV (Linear)

Real-time broadcast delivered over IP. Uses multicast (one stream → many receivers) to avoid multiplying bandwidth per viewer. Relies on IGMP for group membership. Same content, same time — like traditional broadcast.

VOD (Video on Demand)

Viewer selects content from catalogue; unicast stream served from storage. Each viewer gets an individual stream. Requires scalable storage + delivery infrastructure. No broadcast efficiency — every viewer = one stream.

Time-Shift / Catch-Up TV

Recording of live channels on server-side (nPVR) or STB-based (cPVR). Viewers can pause, rewind, and catch up on programmes. Requires large NAS/SAN storage and careful rights management per channel.

Start-Over & Look-Back

Start-Over lets viewer restart a live programme from the beginning. Look-Back (7-day replay) stores a rolling buffer of all channels. Often combined in hospitality IPTV as premium features.

MATV → IPTV Transition Architecture

Hybrid MATV+IPTV headend — RF feeds an IP encoder; multicast streams go to managed network; STBs and smart devices receive over LAN

Unicast vs Multicast vs Broadcast
MethodHow It WorksBandwidth ScalingIPTV Use
UnicastOne stream per viewer (separate TCP/UDP flow)Linear — 100 viewers = 100× bandwidthVOD, catch-up, OTT (Netflix model)
MulticastOne stream replicated by network switches/routers using IGMPConstant — 100 viewers = same bandwidth as 1Live TV in managed IPTV headend
BroadcastAll devices receive all traffic (Layer 2 flood)Constant but wastes STB CPU on unwanted streamsNot used in IPTV (poor practice)
Pro Tip — Multicast Requires a Managed Network

Consumer switches and unmanaged hardware do NOT support IGMP snooping — they flood multicast to all ports, saturating every link. Managed switches with IGMP snooping enabled contain multicast traffic to only the ports that have joined the group. This is non-negotiable for IPTV live TV.

IPTV Protocols at a Glance
RTP/UDP (Live TV)
Real-time Transport Protocol over UDP — low latency multicast delivery of MPEG-TS. No retransmission; brief packet loss causes artefacts. Used on managed LANs with QoS.
RTSP (Control)
Real-Time Streaming Protocol — used for play/pause/seek control of unicast streams (e.g., VOD). Like a VCR remote over the network — it controls an RTP session.
HLS (HTTP Live Streaming)
Apple's adaptive bitrate protocol — video segmented into .ts chunks (typically 2–10s each) served via HTTP. Works over internet/OTT. Higher latency (10–30s) vs RTP.
DASH (MPEG-DASH)
ISO standard equivalent to HLS. Codec-agnostic (H.264, HEVC, AV1). Widely used for OTT streaming (Netflix, Disney+). Requires DRM integration (Widevine, PlayReady).

Redundancy & Resilience

N+1 Redundancy

One spare encoder/IRD for every N active units. Failover triggered by heartbeat loss. 1+1 protection = automatic switchover in <1 second. Critical for SLA-bearing hotel systems where channel loss = guest complaints.

Input Redundancy

Dual satellite feeds from two dishes at different azimuths, or satellite + terrestrial for the same channels. Automatic switching on signal loss. Dual-power supplies in all critical rack equipment. UPS protecting entire headend.

⚠ Single Point of Failure — IP Switch

Many installers spec redundant encoders but forget the switch. A single unmanaged or unconfigured switch failure takes down the entire IPTV service. Use managed switches with link aggregation (LACP) and spanning tree, and consider a hot-standby spare switch for critical installs.

Stage 8 of 16

Video Encoding & Compression

Video encoding is the science of making video files small enough to transmit without losing perceptible quality. Modern codecs deliver UHD 4K content at bitrates where older standards struggled with SD. Understanding compression is critical for bandwidth planning and quality management.

Codec Evolution — Quality Ladder

Each generation delivers ~50% bandwidth saving at equal quality — enabling the jump from SD to 4K on the same infrastructure

How Video Compression Works — Key Concepts
Intra-frame (I-frame)
Independently encoded full picture — no reference to other frames. Highest quality and data size. Required for random access (seeking). H.264/HEVC IDR frames. Typically every 1–2 seconds (GOP length).
Inter-frame (P & B frames)
Predicted from reference frames — only differences are coded. P-frames reference forward; B-frames reference forward and backward. Much smaller than I-frames (10–50× smaller for static content).
DCT / Transform Coding
Discrete Cosine Transform converts pixel blocks to frequency domain — high-frequency components (fine detail) discarded at controlled quality levels. This is where compression artefacts (blockiness) originate.
GOP (Group of Pictures)
I-frame followed by P and B frames. Longer GOP = better compression, higher latency, slower channel change. Live TV: GOP 1–2s. VOD: GOP 2–4s. HEVC: larger GOPs possible due to CTU coding tree.

HEVC/H.265 requires patent royalty payments — typically per-encoder or per-stream. In a 200-room hotel encoding 50 channels in HEVC, licensing can add thousands per year. Always factor HEVC royalties into total cost of ownership. AV1 is royalty-free but hardware support on legacy STBs is limited.

Stage 9 of 16

IPTV Network Architecture

IPTV lives or dies by the network. Unlike web browsing (which tolerates delay), live TV is a real-time service — a single congested link or misconfigured switch brings the entire system down for every viewer simultaneously. Network design is as important as headend design.

IGMP — The Foundation of Multicast TV

Internet Group Management Protocol (IGMP) allows STBs to "join" and "leave" multicast groups (channels). Switches use IGMP snooping to forward each multicast stream only to ports that have joined it.

IGMP snooping on floor switches ensures multicast streams only reach rooms that have joined the group — critical for network efficiency

IGMP v2
Join/Leave messages. Leave group sent to all-routers address (224.0.0.2). Widely supported. Slower leave latency (~last member query interval).
IGMP v3
Source-Specific Multicast (SSM) — join a specific source within a group. Required for IGMPv3-capable IPTV systems. Faster channel change. Preferred for new deployments.
IGMP Querier
One device per VLAN must act as IGMP querier — periodically sends Membership Queries to refresh group lists. Usually the Layer 3 switch or router. Misconfigured querier = channels disappearing after a few minutes.
VLAN Design for IPTV

VLAN 20 — VOD / UnicastOn-demand contentUDP/TCP unicast (RTSP/HLS)Bandwidth-managed; per-stream policing

⚠ IPTV & Guest Internet on the Same VLAN

Never put IPTV streams on the same VLAN as guest internet. A guest torrenting at maximum speed will saturate the link and disrupt all IPTV streams simultaneously. VLANs with QoS are mandatory — IPTV traffic must be classified as Expedited Forwarding (DSCP EF, PHB 46).

QoS & Bandwidth Planning
IPTV Bandwidth Planning Formula
Required bandwidth = (Number of simultaneous channels × Bitrate per channel) + (VOD peak users × VOD bitrate) + 30% headroom
Example: 60 channels × 6 Mbit/s = 360 Mbit/s + 50 VOD users × 8 Mbit/s = 400 Mbit/s + 30% = 988 Mbit/s ≈ 1 Gbit/s core link

QoS Classification (DSCP)

  • IPTV Live (RTP): EF (DSCP 46) — highest priority
  • VOD (RTSP): AF41 (DSCP 34) — assured forwarding
  • Middleware (TCP): CS3 (DSCP 24)
  • Guest internet: BE (DSCP 0) — best effort
  • Bandwidth policing: 50 Mbit/s cap per room port

Multicast Fast Leave

When a guest changes channel, the STB sends an IGMP Leave. Without Fast Leave, the switch waits for the last member query response before stopping the old stream — adding 1–3s of delay. Enable IGMP Fast Leave on all edge ports to achieve sub-second channel change.

Stage 10 of 16

Middleware & Software Platforms

Middleware is the intelligence layer of an IPTV system — it manages the EPG, STB provisioning, DRM licensing, billing, and the user interface. Choosing the right middleware defines the user experience and the operational cost of the system for years.

What Middleware Does

Middleware is the hub connecting EPG, DRM, VOD, billing, analytics, and STB management — it orchestrates the entire guest experience

EPG — Electronic Programme Guide

EPG Data Sources

  • DVB EIT: Embedded in the TS — 7-day guide for all services. Extracted by headend.
  • XMLTV: XML format EPG data from online providers (Rovi, TVTV, Gracenote). Richer metadata, extended schedule.
  • DVB SI: NIT/SDT provides channel names and bouquets for the channel list.
  • Middleware EPG editor: Manual overrides for local/branded channel names.

EPG Display Standards

  • HbbTV (Hybrid Broadcast Broadband TV) — European standard for interactive EPG on smart TVs
  • MHEG-5 — UK legacy interactive TV standard (Freeview)
  • Custom middleware UI — proprietary STB-based guide
  • HTML5 / React-based — modern middleware browser-rendered EPG
  • Resolution: Typically 7-day guide, 30-min granularity minimum

VOD — Video on Demand

VOD transforms a passive TV system into an interactive library of thousands of titles. Understanding the complete content pipeline — from ingest to viewer delivery — is essential for designing scalable, cost-effective VOD platforms.

VOD Pipeline: Ingest → Transcode → Store → Deliver

Complete VOD pipeline — each stage adds value and cost; design choices here affect quality, storage, and bandwidth bills for the life of the system

CDN — Content Delivery Network

Why Use a CDN for VOD?

A CDN caches video segments at edge nodes close to viewers. Instead of every viewer pulling from one origin server, the CDN serves from nearby caches. Reduces origin bandwidth, improves start time and buffering, and scales to millions of viewers without headend changes.

CDN vs Local Origin (Hotel)

In hospitality, a local VOD origin (NAS on LAN) is often better than a cloud CDN — no WAN dependency, lower latency, and no per-GB egress charges. Cloud CDN makes sense for multi-property hotel chains sharing a single content catalogue.

VOD Storage Sizing
Storage Sizing Formula
Storage (TB) = Catalogue size (hours) × Avg bitrate (Mbit/s) × 0.45 (GB per hour per Mbit/s) × Number of quality levels
Example: 500 titles × 1.5 hr avg × 5 Mbit/s (H.264 HD) = 3.375 TB for 1 quality level. With 3 quality levels (SD/HD/FHD): × 3 = ~10 TB. Add 40% for thumbnails, metadata, transcoding workspace → ~14 TB.
⚠ nPVR Storage Growth — Unplanned Capacity

Network PVR (server-side recording) where guests can record live TV is a major hidden storage cost. A 100-room hotel with 7-day catch-up for 50 channels at 3 Mbit/s = 50 × 7 × 24 × 3 × 0.45 GB = 11.3 TB rolling. Every time you add a channel or extend look-back, storage grows linearly. Size for 18 months of catalogue growth.

DRM & Content Security

Content owners will not license their premium movies and live sports without DRM (Digital Rights Management). Understanding how DRM works — and which system to choose for your devices — is essential for any commercial IPTV or VOD deployment.

How DRM Works — The Basics

DRM flow: content encrypted at source → viewer requests licence → key server validates and issues CEK → device decrypts in Trusted Execution Environment

Multi-DRM — The Industry Standard

Since no single DRM covers all devices, professional IPTV deployments use multi-DRM: the same encrypted content is packaged once (CENC — Common Encryption), and licence keys are served by a multi-DRM service (EZDRM, BuyDRM, Vualto, Irdeto, Nagra) that can issue Widevine, PlayReady, or FairPlay licences to whichever device is requesting.

Widevine Security Levels
Widevine L1 — Highest Security
Decryption and content rendering in Trusted Execution Environment (TEE)
Required for 4K/UHD content by major studios (Netflix, Disney+, Amazon)
Required for HD (1080p) content from premium services
Widevine L2 — Medium Security
Decryption in TEE, rendering in normal processor
Rarely deployed — transitional level
Widevine L3 — Software Only
All decryption and rendering in software (no TEE)
Allows SD only for most premium content. Acceptable for hospitality SD VOD
MAG STBs typically L3 — limits content licensing options
⚠ DRM Level Affects Content Licensing

Studios specify minimum security level per content tier. A hotel specifying cheap MAG STBs (Widevine L3) cannot then license 4K movies from major studios — the DRM level blocks it. Always define content ambitions first, then work backwards to specify devices that support the required DRM security level.

Common Encryption (CENC) & Multi-DRM Services

CENC — ISO/IEC 23001-7

Common Encryption allows one encrypted media file to be decrypted by multiple DRM systems (Widevine, PlayReady). Both use the same AES-128 encrypted segments; only the licence acquisition differs. Eliminates need to store separate encrypted files per DRM.

Multi-DRM Providers

  • EZDRM: Simple API, per-licence pricing, Widevine + PlayReady + FairPlay
  • BuyDRM (KeyOS): Enterprise multi-DRM SaaS
  • Vualto (Castlabs): Full packaging + multi-DRM; hospitality focus
  • Irdeto: Broadcast + OTT DRM; used by major telcos
  • Nagra: Premium CAS + DRM; carrier-grade

STB Provisioning Methods
DHCP Option 66
STB boots, gets IP via DHCP, DHCP option 66 provides TFTP/HTTP server URL. STB downloads bootstrap config file (channel list, middleware URL, credentials). Zero-touch provisioning — plug in, auto-configures.

Legacy STBs (MAG 250/322) boot via TFTP. Server provides a portal.conf file with middleware URL, NTP, and DNS settings. TFTP must be accessible on management VLAN from all STB ports.

Enterprise Android TV management: Google's managed devices API, or vendor MDM (Amino's cloud, Formuler's cloud). Central app deployment, policy enforcement, remote wipe. Essential for 100+ room properties.
⚠ MAC Address Binding vs Room Number

In hotels, STBs are mapped to room numbers — not MAC addresses — in the middleware. If a STB is replaced, the room mapping must be reassigned. If STBs don't have printed MAC addresses, provisioning becomes a site-wide manual exercise. Always label STBs with room number at install time and maintain a MAC-to-room database.

Licensing Models — Know Before You Quote
Per-Room Perpetual
One-time software licence per room/STB. Higher upfront cost, no recurring fee. Example: middleware licence £150/room × 200 rooms = £30,000 one-time. Popular for owned properties.
Per-Room SaaS (Annual)
Monthly or annual subscription per room. Lower CapEx, predictable OpEx. Example: £8/room/month × 200 rooms = £1,600/month (£19,200/year). Includes updates and support. Popular for managed/leased properties.
Per-Channel Encoder Licence
Headend encoder licences often charged per simultaneous channel. Example: 60-channel licence at £500/channel = £30,000. HEVC may carry additional patent royalties on top.
Per-Concurrent-Stream VOD
VOD platforms sometimes charge per simultaneous stream (peak viewing). Must size licence for peak (e.g., 30% of rooms concurrently for a 200-room hotel = 60 concurrent stream licences).
Content Licensing (AVLS)
Hotel TV content rights are separate from software licences. AVLS (Audio-Visual Licence and Services) or SCAD-like agreements in many markets. Live TV retransmission rights must be specifically licensed for hotels (not covered by domestic broadcast licences).
⚠ Content Rights Are Not Included — Ever

A common client misconception: "we have satellite subscription at home, can we use it for the hotel?" NO. Domestic broadcast licences explicitly prohibit commercial distribution. Hotels must obtain specific hospitality content licences from broadcasters or content aggregators (e.g., Sky for Business, Freesat for Business, Hotel Channels). This cost is separate from, and often larger than, the IPTV system cost.

Grand Metropolitan — IPTV Bandwidth Worksheet
Live TV: 80 channels × 5 Mbit/s (H.264 HD CBR) = 400 Mbit/s (multicast — fixed regardless of viewers)

Mgmt/EPG: ~10 Mbit/s
