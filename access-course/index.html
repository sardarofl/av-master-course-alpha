Stage 1

What Is Access Control?

Foundations, the three factors of authentication, and why it matters

Welcome to Access Control

Access control is the technology and practice of deciding who can go where, and when. It sits at the intersection of physical security, IT infrastructure, and compliance law. Master it and you become one of the most valuable people on any security project.

🏛️ The Physical Security Layer Model

No single technology secures a building. Access control is one layer in a stack — each layer compensates for the weaknesses of the others.

🔑 The Three Factors of Authentication

🧠 Something You KNOW

PIN codes, passwords, passphrases. The cheapest factor to deploy — but can be shared, forgotten, or shoulder-surfed. Never use alone for high-security areas.

Risk: Shareable, forgettable

Cost: Near zero

Use: Low security + 2nd factor

💳 Something You HAVE

Key cards, fobs, mobile credentials. The most common factor in commercial access control. A card can be cloned, lost, or lent — but is far harder to share than a PIN.

Risk: Lost/stolen/cloned

Cost: Low–Medium

Use: Most commercial deployments

👁️ Something You ARE

Fingerprint, face, iris, vein. The strongest single factor — cannot be forgotten or lent. Highest cost and most complex to deploy. Privacy implications in many countries.

Risk: Spoofing (high-end attacks)

Cost: High

Use: High-security, data centres

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Combining two or more factors multiplies security exponentially. Card + PIN is the most common commercial MFA combination. Biometric + card is used for data centres and government sites.

💰 The Real Cost of Getting It Wrong

Financial

Direct Losses

  • Theft of assets, stock, or equipment
  • Insurance premium increases after a breach
  • Emergency locksmith calls after break-in
  • GDPR fines for data breaches (up to 4% revenue)
Operational

Indirect Losses

  • Business disruption during investigation
  • Legal liability if a third party is harmed
  • Reputational damage — clients lose trust
  • Staff safety incidents in unsecured areas

⚠ Common Blind Spot

Most clients focus on external threats (intruders) and forget that insider threats account for over 60% of security incidents. A proper access control system creates an audit trail that deters and detects internal theft just as effectively as it deters break-ins.

Stage 1 Key Takeaways

  • Access control is one layer in a full security stack — it doesn't work in isolation
  • Three authentication factors: Know, Have, Are — combine them for MFA
  • The cost of a breach is always higher than the cost of prevention
  • Audit trails from access control deter insider threats — not just intruders
  • Your job is to match the right technology to the client's risk profile and budget
Stage 2

System Types & Architectures

Standalone, networked, IP-based, cloud — choosing the right architecture for every project

Architecture Defines Everything

The architecture you choose on day one determines the cost, scalability, resilience, and management model for the entire system life. Get this wrong and the client will be calling you in 18 months for a rip-and-replace.

🏗️ The Four Architecture Types

🔒 Standalone

1–5 Doors

A self-contained lock with built-in controller. No network. No server. Credentials programmed locally by button sequence or small software dongle.

Cost: £100–£400/door

Mgmt: Local, manual

Resilience: Excellent (no dependencies)

Best for: Small offices, storage rooms, single-tenant properties.

🖥️ Controller-Based

5–500 Doors

Readers connect via RS-485 or Wiegand to a door controller. Controllers connect to an on-premise server via TCP/IP. Central management of all doors.

Cost: £300–£800/door

Mgmt: Server software on-site

Resilience: Good (local controller fallback)

Best for: Medium–large commercial buildings, multi-floor offices.

🌐 IP / PoE-Based

2–200 Doors

Each reader or door controller connects directly to the network via Cat6. Powered by PoE. Decision-making can be local (edge) or centralised.

Cost: £400–£900/door

Mgmt: Browser or app

Resilience: Depends on design

Best for: Modern builds, retrofit projects with good network infrastructure.

☁️ Cloud-Managed

Any Size

Hardware connects to vendor cloud platform. All management, reporting, and configuration done via web browser or mobile app. No on-site server.

Cost: £200–£600/door + monthly SaaS

Mgmt: Cloud portal, any device

Resilience: Needs internet + local fallback

Best for: Multi-site businesses, MSP-managed estates, clients who want minimal IT overhead.

⚠ Blind Spot — Cloud Without Local Fallback

Cloud-managed systems are convenient — but if the internet goes down, what happens to your doors? Always confirm with the vendor whether the controller has local credential caching. If it doesn't, a simple internet outage can lock out an entire building. Always spec a local fallback mode for any cloud deployment.

Stage 2 Key Takeaways

  • Match architecture to site size, IT capability, and budget — not to your favourite brand
  • Standalone for small, controller-based for medium-large, cloud for multi-site managed estates
  • Always confirm offline resilience on cloud systems — missing this costs you a client
  • Ongoing SaaS cost must be built into the client's TCO from day one
  • Controllers should cache credentials locally for at least 24–48 hours offline operation
Stage 3

Credential Technologies

Proximity, smart cards, mobile credentials, biometrics — security vs. convenience vs. cost

The Credential Is the Key

The credential is what grants or denies access — it can be a card, a fob, a smartphone, or a fingerprint. Understanding the technology behind each one lets you specify the right level of security for each zone, and spot vulnerabilities before they become incidents.

📡 Proximity — 125 kHz (Legacy)

Security Risk

How It Works

A passive coil in the card harvests energy from the reader's RF field and broadcasts a fixed, unencrypted ID number. The reader passes this number to the controller unchanged.

  • Frequency: 125 kHz
  • Range: 2–15 cm
  • Standards: EM4100, HID Prox, Indala
  • Encryption: None
Still Deployed Everywhere

The Problem

125 kHz credentials broadcast their ID in plain text. A £30 card cloner from Amazon can read and copy any proximity card within 10 cm — silently, in under a second.

⚠ Still Being Installed in 2024

Thousands of installers still fit 125 kHz readers because they're cheap. If your client has proximity cards, tell them: their system can be bypassed for £30.

💎 Smart Cards — 13.56 MHz (Current Standard)

MIFARE Classic

Freq: 13.56 MHz (ISO 14443A)

Encryption: Proprietary CRYPTO1 — BROKEN

Clone time: < 60 seconds

Still the most installed smart card globally. The encryption was cracked in 2008 and tools to clone it are freely available. Do not spec for new installations.

MIFARE DESFire EV2/EV3

Freq: 13.56 MHz (ISO 14443A)

Encryption: AES-128

Mutual auth: Yes

The current gold standard for smart cards. AES-128 encrypted, mutual authentication between card and reader, cannot be cloned with off-the-shelf tools. Specify this for all new systems.

HID iCLASS SE / Seos

Freq: 13.56 MHz

Encryption: AES-128

Mobile: Yes (HID Mobile Access)

HID's proprietary high-security card. Seos supports mobile credentials natively. Strong choice where HID infrastructure is already deployed.

📱 Mobile Credentials

Growing Fast

Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE)

Smartphone communicates with reader via BLE. Works with phone in pocket or bag. Range typically 5–10 cm (tap mode) or up to 30 cm (hands-free).

  • No card to lose, issue, or cancel — done via app
  • Instantly revoke lost devices remotely
  • Multi-site access from one credential
  • Requires reader upgrade and app infrastructure
Premium

NFC & Apple/Google Wallet

Credential stored directly in Apple Wallet or Google Wallet. No app needed — tap phone or watch to reader. Works even with dead battery (iPhone Express Mode).

  • Most frictionless user experience available
  • Apple UWB (Ultra Wideband) — hands-free unlock as you approach
  • Requires reader hardware that supports NFC wallet credentials
  • Currently supported by HID, ASSA ABLOY, Allegion

Stage 3 Key Takeaways

  • 125 kHz proximity = cloneable in seconds. Never spec it for new builds
  • MIFARE Classic encryption is broken — treat it like proximity
  • DESFire EV2/EV3 with AES-128 is the minimum for any new commercial system
  • Mobile credentials are the future — but require reader infrastructure investment
  • Biometrics: high security but expensive, privacy law implications, and require fallback
  • MFA (card + PIN, card + biometric) is the highest security tier available
Stage 4

Readers & Input Devices

Wiegand vs OSDP, form factors, REX devices, door position switches — wiring every component correctly

The Reader Is the Front Door of Your System

The reader is what the user touches every single day. It must be reliable, tamper-resistant, and — critically — communicate with the controller over a secure protocol. The choice between Wiegand and OSDP is one of the most important decisions you'll make on any project.

⚡ Wiegand vs OSDP — The Critical Choice

💡 OSDP — SIA Standard

OSDP (Open Supervised Device Protocol) v2 is the Security Industry Association standard for reader-to-controller communication. It uses RS-485 at up to 115,200 baud with AES-128 encryption. Any new installation should use OSDP wherever the controller supports it. Wire-tapping a Wiegand line is a real, documented attack vector.

📦 Reader Form Factors

🔲 Mullion Reader

Slim profile, fits on door frame edge. Ideal where wall space is limited. Standard for most internal doors.

🔳 Single-Gang Reader

Fits UK/EU back box. Larger face plate, often includes PIN pad. Standard commercial interior installation.

🌧️ Weatherproof / Outdoor

IP65 rated. Anti-vandal stainless bezel. Heated (for frost). Required for any external door — check IP rating before speccing.

🛡️ Vandal-Resistant

IK10 rated (10 joules impact). Solid metal body. Required for car parks, service entries, high-risk public areas.

🃏 Multi-Tech Reader

Reads 125 kHz AND 13.56 MHz AND BLE simultaneously. Essential for migration projects — allows phased card upgrade without reader swap.

📟 Keypad / PIN + Card

Combines card reader with PIN entry for MFA. Weatherproof versions available. Backlit keys for low-light areas.

🚪 Input Devices — REX & Door Position

REX — Request to Exit

Exit Devices

Allows free egress without a credential. The REX signal tells the controller "someone is legitimately exiting" — suppressing the door-forced alarm.

  • PIR REX: Passive infrared — hands-free, detects body heat
  • Push Button REX: Simple, reliable, cheap — but requires intentional press
  • Touch Bar / Touchplate: Capacitive or mechanical — ADA compliant options
  • Under-carpet mat: Weight-activated, invisible installation

⚠ Blind Spot — REX Coverage

PIR REX must cover the full egress path. A poorly positioned PIR triggers the door alarm every time someone exits. Always test full coverage before commissioning.

DPS — Door Position Switch

Door Monitoring

A magnetic contact that tells the controller whether the door is open or closed. Essential for "door held open" and "door forced" alarms.

  • Surface mount or concealed in door frame
  • NC (Normally Closed) — alarm on open circuit
  • Armoured cable for high-security doors
  • Required on every monitored door in the schedule

Door Held Open Alert: Controller triggers alarm if door stays open > X seconds after valid credential (configurable, typically 15–30 seconds).

Door Forced Alert: Door opened without valid credential or REX — immediate alarm.

Stage 4 Key Takeaways

  • OSDP v2 over RS-485 is the correct choice for any new installation — Wiegand is a security liability
  • Wiegand wire-tapping is a real attack — OSDP encrypts the reader-to-controller link
  • Match reader form factor to environment: IP65 outside, IK10 for at-risk areas
  • Multi-tech readers allow phased migration from legacy 125 kHz to smart card/mobile
  • REX coverage must be tested — a missed PIR zone means door-held alarms every shift
  • DPS on every monitored door — without it, you can't alarm forced entries
Stage 5

Lock Types & Door Hardware

Electric strikes, maglocks, electrified mortise, panic hardware — fail-safe vs fail-secure

The Lock Is Where Access Control Meets Reality

The most sophisticated controller in the world is useless if the wrong lock is on the door. Understanding locking hardware — how it works, when it engages, and how it behaves on power loss — is what separates a professional installer from someone who just hangs readers.

🔒 Fail-Safe vs Fail-Secure — The Most Important Decision

⚠️ Fail-Secure locks MUST NOT be installed on fire escape routes unless combined with a fire alarm interface that releases them on alarm. This is a life safety code violation.

🏗️ Lock Types Explained

⚡ Electric Strike

Most Common

Replaces the mechanical strike plate. The latch or deadlatch from a standard lock engages — the strike releases electrically to allow the door to open.

Fail: Fail-safe OR fail-secure versions

Voltage: 12V or 24V DC

Current: 300mA–800mA

Standard: ANSI, CE, EN 14846

Best for: Office doors, corridors, apartment entries. Works with existing door hardware.

🧲 Magnetic Lock (Maglock)

Fire Door — Caution

An electromagnet holds an armature plate. No moving parts — just magnetic force. Always fail-safe (releases on power loss).

Holding Force: 300 lb (136 kg) or 600 lb (272 kg)

Voltage: 12V or 24V DC

Current: 250mA–500mA

Fail mode: Always FAIL-SAFE

Requires: Door closer (mandatory), REX on exit side, DPS.

🔐 Electrified Mortise Lock

High Security

Full mortise lock body with electrified latch or deadbolt. Highest mechanical security — the lock cylinder is supplemented by electrical control.

Fail: Fail-safe or fail-secure

Voltage: 12V or 24V DC

Grade: ANSI Grade 1, EN 12209

Best for: Main entrances, executive areas, anywhere requiring both mechanical strength and electronic control.

🚪 Electrified Panic Hardware

Fire Egress

Panic bar (push bar) with electrical control. The bar always provides free egress — the electrical component controls entry only. UL listed for fire doors.

Standard: EN 1125, UL 10C

Fail: Always allows egress

Brands: Von Duprin, ASSA ABLOY, Dormakaba

Best for: Fire exits, stairwell doors, warehouse exits requiring both security and fire compliance.

⚠ Blind Spot — Maglocks on Fire Doors

A maglock installed on a fire escape door without a fire alarm interface is a life safety violation in most jurisdictions. The fire alarm must trigger a relay that cuts power to the maglock — releasing it instantly. Always confirm fire alarm integration is in scope and tested before sign-off.

Stage 5 Key Takeaways

  • Fail-safe = unlocks on power loss. Required on all fire egress routes
  • Fail-secure = stays locked on power loss. Server rooms, vaults, high-security areas only
  • Maglocks are always fail-safe — but still require door closer and fire alarm interface
  • Electric strike is the most versatile — available in both fail modes
  • Panic hardware on fire doors must always allow free egress regardless of power state
  • Check the relevant standard for the jurisdiction before specifying
Stage 6

Power & Fail Modes

12V vs 24V, PSU sizing, battery backup, voltage drop, fire alarm interface

Power Is the Foundation of Reliability

Most access control failures trace back to a power problem: undersized PSU, voltage drop over long cable runs, flat batteries, or no fire alarm interface. Get power right and your system will run for years without a call-back.

⚡ 12V vs 24V DC

12V DC

Standard Choice

Most common in smaller systems. Most readers and low-power strikes run natively at 12V. Simpler, cheaper components.

  • Most readers: 12V DC, 100–300mA
  • Most electric strikes: 12V DC, 300–600mA
  • Smaller PSU, lower cost
  • More voltage drop over long runs
24V DC

Better for Long Runs

Preferred for larger systems and long cable runs. Same power at double the voltage means half the current — voltage drop is significantly reduced.

  • Maglocks: often 24V for lower current draw
  • Long cable runs: 24V halves voltage drop
  • Modern PoE readers: 48V (controlled by switch)
  • Requires 24V-rated components throughout

🔋 PSU Sizing — Step by Step

🔋 Battery Backup Sizing

EN 50131 Grade 2 systems require 12 hours standby. Grade 3 requires 24 hours. Calculate battery capacity (Ah) using:

Battery Capacity (Ah) = Load Current (A) × Standby Hours × 1.25 (derating factor)

Example: 3.4A load × 12 hours × 1.25 = 51Ah minimum battery
Use 2× 7Ah sealed lead-acid batteries for short backup, or dedicated 40–60Ah for full EN50131 compliance.

🔥 Fire Alarm Interface

How It Works

The fire alarm panel has a volt-free relay output. When a fire alarm activates, this relay switches and cuts power (or signals) to specific access control locks — releasing fail-safe locks and granting free egress throughout the building.

  • Relay wired into lock PSU or controller input
  • Fire alarm signal = release all fire exit locks
  • Some systems: fire alarm = lock down server room
  • Must be tested as part of fire drill procedure

Voltage Drop Formula

For long cable runs, calculate voltage drop to ensure the lock receives sufficient voltage:

Vdrop = (2 × L × I × R) / 1000
L = cable run (metres), I = current (A), R = resistance (Ω/km for chosen cable)

Rule of thumb: No more than 10% voltage drop at the lock. Use 1.5mm² cable for runs over 50m at 12V.

1.0mm² cable: 18.1 Ω/km (each conductor)

1.5mm² cable: 12.1 Ω/km

2.5mm² cable: 7.41 Ω/km

⚠ Blind Spot — Undersized PSU

The most common access control fault call: locks clicking randomly, cards randomly rejected, system randomly rebooting. Always the PSU. Installers calculate the static load but forget that 4 locks activating simultaneously creates a peak inrush current that's 3–5× the standing load. Size your PSU for the peak, not the average.

Stage 6 Key Takeaways

  • Always add 25% safety margin to calculated PSU load
  • 24V systems reduce voltage drop on long cable runs
  • Battery backup must meet EN 50131 grade requirements for the installation
  • Fire alarm interface is mandatory on all fail-safe locks serving fire escape routes
  • Calculate voltage drop for runs over 30m — undersized cable = unreliable locks
  • Peak inrush (multiple locks firing at once) is always higher than standing load — design for the worst case
Stage 7

Controllers & Panels

Single-door to enterprise controllers, I/O points, offline mode, major platforms

The Controller Is the Brain

The controller makes the decision: credential presented → is it valid? → is access permitted at this time? → activate the lock. Everything else — readers, locks, servers — feeds into or acts on the controller's decision.

🖥️ Controller Types by Scale

🚪 Single-Door Controller

Doors: 1

Readers: 1–2 (in/out)

I/O: 2–4 inputs, 1–2 relays

Interface: TCP/IP or RS-485

Ideal for PoE deployment — one device per door. Higher unit cost but easier to cable. Used heavily in IP-based systems.

🏢 Multi-Door Panel (4/8/16)

Doors: 4, 8, or 16

Readers: 8–32 via RS-485 bus

I/O: 16–64 inputs, 8–32 relays

Interface: TCP/IP to server

The standard for medium-to-large installations. Mounted in comms room, connects to readers via RS-485 daisy chain. Cost-effective per door at scale.

🏭 Enterprise Controller

Doors: 32–256+

Readers: Via sub-controllers

I/O: Hundreds of points

Interface: Redundant TCP/IP

Used in campus environments, airports, data centres. Often includes hardware redundancy and Tier 1 software (Lenel, Genetec, Honeywell). High cost, full feature set.

📋 I/O Points — Inputs and Outputs

Inputs (Supervised)

Inputs monitor the state of connected devices. "Supervised" means the circuit is continuously monitored — a tampered or cut wire is detected and alarmed.

  • Door Position Switch (DPS) — open/closed
  • REX button / PIR state
  • Tamper switch on reader or enclosure
  • Fire alarm input (from FA panel relay)
  • Intrusion alarm zone input
  • Break-glass unit input

Outputs (Relay)

Relay outputs switch external devices on/off. Volt-free contacts allow control of high-current or high-voltage loads via external power supply.

  • Lock strike / maglock activation
  • Barrier arm or turnstile release
  • Alarm output to monitoring centre
  • Elevator floor relay (access per floor)
  • CCTV camera trigger (alarm-linked recording)
  • Door open indicator light

🔴 Critical: Offline Mode & Local Caching

What happens when the server goes offline — network failure, server crash, scheduled maintenance? A controller without local caching defaults to lockout — nobody in, nobody out.

ScenarioNo Local CacheWith Local Cache
Network failureAll doors lock downNormal operation continues
Server restartDoors offline during rebootUnaffected — local decisions
Emergency card issueNot possible offlinePre-cached credentials only
Audit trailEvents lostStored locally, sync on reconnect

Minimum requirement: Controllers must cache at least 10,000 cardholders and 7 days of events locally. Confirm this in the product spec before purchase.

⚠ Blind Spot — No Offline Fallback = Total Lockout

Cloud and server-dependent systems without verified local credential caching will lock everyone out during a network or server outage. Test offline operation before installation acceptance. Pull the network cable and verify all enrolled cards still work.

Stage 7 Key Takeaways

  • Match controller scale to project — single-door for IP systems, multi-door panels for large wired deployments
  • Supervised inputs detect tampered/cut wires — use on all security-critical inputs
  • Local credential caching is non-negotiable — minimum 10,000 cards, 7 days events
  • Test offline mode before handover — pull the network cable and verify operation
  • Platform choice drives long-term support, training, and integration cost — choose wisely
Stage 8

Zone Planning & BOH/FOH Architecture

Security zoning, Back of House vs Front of House, traffic flow, site survey essentials

Plan the Zones Before You Touch a Product

The best access control hardware in the world delivers no value without a clear zone plan. Zone planning is where security engineering meets architecture — and where you earn your professional fee by thinking through what the client hasn't.

🏨 Front of House (FOH) vs Back of House (BOH)

Originally a hospitality concept, FOH/BOH thinking applies to every building type — hotels, offices, hospitals, retail, education. It defines two fundamentally different security cultures operating in the same building.

Front of House

FOH Principles

Convenience first, security second. FOH serves customers, guests, or the public. Friction = lost revenue. Credentials must be easy to use.

  • Lobby, reception, public corridors
  • Meeting rooms, conference facilities
  • Restaurant, gym, spa, retail areas
  • Guest lifts and parking
  • Credential: key card, mobile, fob
  • Audit trail important but not always real-time
Back of House

BOH Principles

Security first, convenience second. BOH is where operations run — assets, data, and staff are concentrated here. Every entry/exit must be logged.

  • Kitchen, laundry, housekeeping stores
  • IT/server room, telecoms room
  • Maintenance areas, plant rooms
  • Loading dock, goods-in areas
  • Staff-only corridors and service lifts
  • Credential: higher security (MFA for IT room)

🔑 Golden Rule: FOH and BOH must NEVER share a credential tier without explicit role-based access control separation.

A guest credential for room 412 must not also open the kitchen, laundry, or server room — even accidentally.

🗺️ Security Zoning: 5-Level Model

ZONE 5 — MAXIMUM SECURITY  Data centre core, vault, armoury — MFA + biometric, 24/7 logging
ZONE 4 — HIGH SECURITY  IT/server room, executive offices, cash room — card + PIN, full audit
ZONE 3 — CONTROLLED  Staff areas, BOH operations, storerooms — card access, timed schedules
ZONE 2 — MONITORED  FOH tenant areas, meeting rooms — card access or PIN, logged entry
ZONE 1 — PUBLIC  Lobby, reception, car park entrance — free or reception-controlled

📋 Site Survey Checklist

Physical Assessment

  • Door schedule: every controlled door listed with type, size, frame material
  • Fire exit identification — fail-safe lock mandatory
  • Cable routes: ceiling void, conduit, riser availability
  • Comms room location for controller installation
  • Power supply locations — distance to each door
  • Existing hardware to retain or replace
  • Outdoor doors: exposure, vandalism risk, IP/IK rating needed

Operational Assessment

  • Number of staff / cardholders to enrol
  • Working hours and shift patterns (for time schedules)
  • Visitor management requirements
  • Anti-passback areas (canteen, car park)
  • Tailgating risk areas (server room, reception)
  • GDPR: data retention policy agreed with client
  • Integration requirements: CCTV, intruder, BMS, HR system

⚠ Blind Spot — The Service Corridor

The crossover point between FOH and BOH — service corridors, service lifts, staff entrances near public areas — is the highest risk zone in the building. It's where a tailgating threat can transition from public to operational areas without detection. These doors are often left on a simple PIN or mechanical key "for convenience." They should be some of the most tightly controlled doors in the building.

Stage 8 Key Takeaways

  • Zone planning before product selection — the plan drives the spec, not the other way around
  • FOH = convenience first; BOH = security first; crossovers = highest risk
  • Five security zones from public (Zone 1) to maximum security (Zone 5)
  • Guest credentials must NEVER accidentally grant BOH access
  • Service corridors and service lifts are consistently under-secured — flag this on every survey
  • Site survey checklist covers physical AND operational requirements — both are needed

🔌 RS-485 Daisy Chain Topology

🏷️ Cable Labelling Standard

Label Format: [BUILDING]-[FLOOR]-[DOOR NUMBER]-[DEVICE TYPE]

Examples: B1-F2-D014-RDR (Building 1, Floor 2, Door 14, Reader)  |  B1-F2-D014-LCK (Lock)  |  B1-F2-D014-REX (REX)  |  B1-F2-D014-DPS (Door Position Switch)

Label at both ends of every cable run — at the device and at the controller/panel termination. Use heat-shrink labels or cable markers rated for the environment (not paper tape).

⚠️ Ground Loops — The Hidden Failure

⚠ Blind Spot — Improper Shield Termination

Cable shields must be earthed at one end only (typically the controller end). Earthing both ends creates a ground loop — an antenna that picks up electrical noise, causing random card read failures that are almost impossible to diagnose. Always confirm shield termination during commissioning with a continuity tester.

Conduit Requirements

  • EMT (steel conduit): Mechanical protection in exposed areas, plant rooms, car parks
  • PVC conduit: Acceptable in concealed dry locations
  • Flexible conduit: Final connection to door frame (allows door opening)
  • Fire-rated containment: FP200 cable or intumescent-lined trunking at fire compartment crossings
  • Separate security cables from mains electrical — 50mm minimum separation or metallic divider

Door Frame Entry

Cable entry through the door frame is the most common installation failure point:

  • Always use flexible hinge conduit (armoured loop cable)
  • Allow sufficient cable loop — doors open 90°+ and flex the cable thousands of times
  • Fire door frames: maintain fire seal around cable penetration
  • Steel door frames: deburr all conduit entries to prevent cable damage

Stage 9 Key Takeaways

  • OSDP RS-485 runs up to 1200m daisy chain — Wiegand is limited to 150m point-to-point
  • RS-485 bus MUST be terminated at the far end — open bus = random card read failures
  • Shield to earth at controller end only — earthing both ends creates ground loops
  • Label every cable at both ends using a consistent naming convention
  • Fire-rated cable (FP200/PH30) is mandatory for fire system interfaces
  • Flexible hinge conduit at door frames — allow for repeated door movement fatigue
Stage 10

Software & Configuration

Cardholders, access levels, time schedules, anti-passback, GDPR, HR integration

The Software Is Where Policy Becomes Reality

Hardware creates the possibility of control. Software delivers the policy. Time schedules, access groups, anti-passback rules, visitor workflows — get these right and the system runs itself. Get them wrong and you'll be answering calls for years.

👤 Core Data Model

🪪 Cardholder

A person in the system. Fields: name, employee ID, department, photo, start date, expiry date, PIN (optional). One cardholder can hold multiple credentials (card + mobile + backup fob).

💳 Credential

The physical or digital token assigned to a cardholder. Card number, facility code, technology type. A credential can be active, suspended, or expired. Lost card: suspend, don't delete — for audit trail integrity.

🏘️ Access Group

A named collection of doors + time schedules. Assign a cardholder to an access group and they inherit all its permissions. Never assign door-by-door — always use groups for maintainability.

⏰ Time Schedule

Defines when an access group is valid. Monday–Friday 07:00–19:00. Weekend 00:00–00:00 (no access). Holidays override to a restricted schedule. Must be reviewed annually.

📊 Access Level Matrix — Example

🔄 Anti-Passback

Anti-passback prevents a cardholder from entering a zone twice without exiting first — stopping credential-sharing ("passback" of a card to a colleague).

Hard Anti-Passback

Access Denied

If the system detects a second entry without a valid exit record, the credential is refused and an alert is generated. Strong security but can frustrate users if the exit reader is missed.

Soft Anti-Passback

Access Granted + Alert

Access is still granted but an alert is logged and the security team is notified. Less disruptive for staff — better for initial deployment before access discipline is established.

⚖️ GDPR & Data Compliance

⚠ GDPR Applies to Access Control Data

Every card transaction is a record of an individual's location at a specific time — this is personal data under GDPR. You and your client must agree on: data retention period (typically 90 days for audit, up to 2 years for compliance), lawful basis for processing, right to access/erasure policy, and data processor agreements if cloud-hosted.

⚠ Blind Spot — Ghost Credentials from Terminated Staff

The most common security vulnerability found in access control audits: active credentials for staff who left the company months or years ago. The HR system issued an offboarding ticket, but nobody told the access control manager. Always spec an HR system integration or a documented manual process — and audit credential lists quarterly.

Stage 10 Key Takeaways

  • Always use access groups — never assign door-by-door permissions to individuals
  • Time schedules are the second layer of access control — right person, right time
  • Anti-passback deters credential sharing — use soft mode for initial rollout
  • Suspended ≠ deleted — retain credentials for audit trail even after a cardholder leaves
  • GDPR: access control event data is personal data — agree retention policy before go-live
  • Ghost credentials are the #1 vulnerability in every access audit — build in a review process
Stage 11

Alerts, Automation & Rules

Event-driven automation, lockdown, interlocks, mustering, two-person rule

A Smart System Responds — Not Just Records

The difference between a passive logging system and an active security platform is automation. Rules that trigger responses to events turn your access control installation from a database into a live security tool — and justify the premium on your quote.

⚡ Event-Driven Automation Examples

📹 Door Forced → Camera PTZ

When "door forced open" alarm triggers on server room door, the PTZ camera covering that corridor automatically pans to the door and begins recording with a time/event stamp. Security team is notified by push notification.

🔒 Last Out → Arm Intruder

When the last valid credential is used to exit the building after 18:00, the intruder alarm system is automatically armed — no need for staff to separately arm the system. Last-in in the morning can auto-disarm.

🌡️ Sensor Input → Restrict Access

A temperature sensor in a data centre room rises above 35°C. The controller restricts access to IT staff only (removing facilities team from the access list) and triggers a maintenance alert. Prevents overcrowding during a cooling failure.

🚨 Threat Level → Lockdown

Security manager triggers threat level escalation from the monitoring station. All external doors automatically lock, access is restricted to security-cleared staff only, and all cameras switch to maximum frame rate recording.

🚪 Interlock (Mantrap / Airlock)

An interlock ensures that only one door in a sequence can be open at a time. Used in server rooms, embassies, pharmacies, and high-value areas to prevent tailgating.

🔥 Mustering & Fire Evacuation

Mustering is using access control to produce a real-time roll-call during fire evacuation. The system reports who has exited (presented a valid exit credential) and who is still unaccounted for inside the building.

  • Requirements: Exit readers on all escape routes, real-time monitoring station
  • Output: "Present at muster" vs "Not yet accounted for" list — live on security dashboard
  • EN 54 compliance: Access control mustering must not impede fire egress — exit readers are advisory, never restrictive
  • Visitor management integration: Visitor sign-in records include in muster count

👥 Two-Person Rule

Two-Person Integrity (TPI): A rule that prevents any single individual from accessing a high-security area alone. Two valid credentials from two different enrolled individuals must be presented within a defined time window. Used in server rooms with classified data, pharmaceutical storage, evidence rooms, and gold/currency vaults. Prevents insider threats where one bad actor acts alone.

Stage 11 Key Takeaways

  • Event-driven automation turns access control from passive to active security
  • Interlock (mantrap) — only one door open at a time — eliminates tailgating in high-security areas
  • Mustering gives life-safety value to access control beyond just locking doors
  • Two-person rule eliminates single-person insider threat in the highest-security areas
  • Last-out/first-in arming integration removes the most common user error in intruder alarm systems
Stage 12

Integration with Other Systems

CCTV, intruder alarms, BMS, elevator control, video intercom, turnstiles

Integration Is Where the Value Multiplies

An access control system in isolation is a door lock with a database. Integrated with CCTV, intruder alarms, and building management systems, it becomes the nervous system of the entire building — and the most defensible budget line in any security proposal.

🌐 Integrated Security Ecosystem

📷 CCTV Integration

  • Event-linked recording: Access denied event → CCTV system starts recording that camera at maximum frame rate
  • Cardholder photo popup: When a card is presented, the VMS shows the cardholder's enrolled photo alongside the live camera image — guard verifies face = card
  • Video at door: Every access door should have a camera covering it — minimum requirement for any corporate security spec
  • Integration protocols: SDK/API (Genetec, Milestone), database-linked (Lenel), ONVIF (basic camera control)

🛗 Elevator Control

Floor-by-floor access restriction using relay outputs from the access controller — each floor button in the lift cab is connected to a relay that energises (enables the button) only when a valid credential has been presented for that floor.

How It Works

  • Reader installed in lift cab
  • Controller output relay per floor
  • Relay energises floor button for ~30 seconds after valid card
  • Executive floor: card required to select floor in lift
  • Car park: separate credential to access basement levels
Blind Spot

Lift Cab Reader

The reader in the lift cab needs power and data cabling that runs through the lift shaft — coordinated with the lift contractor. This is frequently forgotten until fit-out and causes programme delays. Always confirm lift cab reader in the M&E coordination meeting.

🔔 Video Intercom (Door Station)

💡 SIP/VoIP Intercom Integration

Modern video door stations use SIP protocol over IP. They register as a SIP extension on the building's VoIP system — allowing any IP phone or softphone client to answer the door. The door release is triggered via a DTMF tone or web API call. Confirm the door station and access controller are on the same VLAN — SIP intercoms on an isolated VLAN lose their door release capability if the API call can't reach the access controller.

Stage 12 Key Takeaways

  • CCTV + access control is the minimum integration for any corporate installation
  • Cardholder photo verification at the reader is a powerful anti-tailgating control
  • Elevator control requires coordination with the lift contractor — flag this early
  • Video intercom on isolated VLAN loses door release — confirm network topology with IT
  • BMS integration allows HVAC zoning by occupancy — energy saving and security combined
  • Integration adds value to your proposal and justifies higher project margin
Stage 13

Cybersecurity for Access Control

OSDP encryption, network segmentation, firmware updates, default credentials, IEC 62443

Physical Security Is Now a Cyber Target

Access control systems are IP-connected devices. Controllers have web interfaces. Cloud platforms have APIs. Readers run firmware. Every one of these is a potential attack vector — and most access control installations are configured with no cyber hardening at all.

🔐 Why Wiegand Is a Cybersecurity Liability

Wiegand Attack

The Wire-Tap Attack

Wiegand transmits card data as an unencrypted electrical pulse on two wires (D0, D1). An attacker with physical access to the cable — inside a wall, above a ceiling tile, at a junction box — can:

  1. Clip onto the D0/D1 lines with a data logger (£15 device)
  2. Wait for a legitimate card presentation — captures the card data
  3. Replay the captured signal to gain access at any time

Tool cost: <£50. Skill required: minimal.

OSDP v2 Protection

Why OSDP Wins

  • AES-128 encrypted — captured data is useless without the session key
  • Mutual authentication — reader and controller verify each other
  • Tamper detection — the link monitors for cable interference
  • Secure Channel (SC) mode — must be enabled explicitly in configuration

⚠ Enable Secure Channel

OSDP v2 supports encryption — but it's not always enabled by default. Verify Secure Channel mode is active on every reader during commissioning.

🌐 Network Segmentation

Golden Rule: Access control hardware must NEVER be on the same network segment as corporate user workstations. Use a dedicated security VLAN with firewall rules controlling exactly which devices can communicate with the access control server.

🔑 Default Credentials — The Biggest Vulnerability

⚠ Critical — Change Default Passwords Immediately

The majority of installed access control systems are running with factory default credentials on their web interfaces, database, and SSH access. These defaults (admin/admin, admin/1234, etc.) are published in every product manual and indexed by search engines. A scan with Shodan or a simple Google search can reveal your client's system to any attacker within minutes of it going online. Change ALL default credentials as the first step of commissioning. Document the new credentials in a password manager — never in an email or spreadsheet.

📋 Cyber Hardening Checklist

  • All default passwords changed — controller web UI, database, SSH, SNMP community string
  • OSDP Secure Channel enabled on all readers — verify in controller diagnostic log
  • Access control hardware on dedicated VLAN — firewall rules reviewed by IT
  • Unused ports and services disabled on controllers (Telnet, FTP, HTTP → HTTPS only)
  • Firmware update procedure documented — schedule quarterly review
  • TLS certificates valid and renewed — set calendar reminder 60 days before expiry
  • Remote access via VPN only — no public-facing web interfaces
  • Log forwarding to SIEM configured — security events monitored 24/7
  • Physical tamper seals on controller enclosures — tamper event alerts enabled

📜 Relevant Standards

IEC 62443

Industrial Cybersecurity

The primary standard for OT/ICS security. Applicable to access control as an operational technology system. Defines Security Levels 1–4.

NIST SP 800-82

ICS Security Guide

US government guide for industrial control system security. Widely adopted as a reference framework for physical security system cyber hardening.

SIA OSDP Standard

IEC 60839-11-5

The international standard for OSDP. Specifies the protocol, Secure Channel requirements, and compliance testing procedures.

Stage 13 Key Takeaways

  • Wiegand wire-tap is a real, cheap, and simple attack — OSDP with Secure Channel eliminates it
  • Access control must be on a dedicated VLAN — never on the corporate user network
  • Changing default credentials is the single most impactful cyber security action — do it first
  • Remote access via VPN only — no public-facing controller interfaces
  • Firmware updates must be scheduled — outdated firmware = known CVEs waiting to be exploited
Stage 14

Commissioning, Testing & Handover

Point-to-point testing, acceptance protocol, maintenance schedule, troubleshooting matrix

Commissioning Is What You Get Paid For

Installation is craft. Commissioning is engineering. A thorough commissioning process protects you legally, builds client confidence, and eliminates the callback nightmare. Document everything.

✅ Pre-Installation Checklist

  • Door schedule confirmed and signed off by client
  • Access level matrix approved by security manager
  • Time schedules confirmed for all staff groups
  • Fire alarm interface relay confirmed with FA contractor
  • Network VLAN created and switch ports configured (IT signoff)
  • PSU capacity calculated and verified for each controller
  • Cable schedule complete — all runs labelled at both ends
  • Controller enclosures installed, earthed, and locked

🧪 Point-to-Point Testing Protocol

Test every door individually before client demonstration. Use this sequence for each door:

  1. Card read test: Present valid card → door unlocks. Present invalid/unknown card → door stays locked, access denied event logged
  2. REX test: Activate REX device (press button / wave at PIR) → door unlocks without alarm. Verify REX event appears in audit log
  3. Door held open: Open door with valid card, hold open past timer → door held open alarm triggers. Confirm alarm in software
  4. Door forced test: Open door without card or REX → door forced alarm triggers immediately. Confirm alarm in software
  5. DPS state: Verify door open/closed status displays correctly in monitoring software
  6. Time schedule test: Verify card is rejected outside permitted hours
  7. Fire alarm interface: Confirm lock releases within 3 seconds of simulated fire alarm signal

A system handed over without proper client training generates 70% of all post-installation support calls. Train at minimum two members of client staff on: issuing and cancelling credentials, running access reports, responding to alarms, and resetting the system after a fire alarm. Document the training session. This protects you and reduces callbacks.

Stage 15

Hotel & Hospitality Lock Systems

Hotel lock technology, key hierarchy, encoder systems, online vs offline, mobile keys

Hotels Are a World of Their Own

Hotel access control is fundamentally different from commercial access control. Credentials are disposable — issued at check-in, invalid at check-out. Locks are predominantly offline. The key hierarchy spans dozens of access levels. And the stakes of a failure are immediate: a guest locked out of their room at 2am is a TripAdvisor review waiting to happen.

🔑 What Makes Hotel Locks Unique

📆 Disposable Credentials

Every check-in creates a new credential valid only for that room, for that specific date range. At check-out (or at the encoded expiry time), the credential automatically becomes invalid. No card returns required — the system handles expiry.

📡 Predominantly Offline

Guest room locks store their own access rules locally. They do not need a live connection to the PMS or server to grant access. This dramatically reduces cabling cost and complexity — but limits real-time audit capability.

📈 Massive Scale

A 200-room hotel has 200+ guest locks plus all BOH, common area, and management locks — potentially 300–400 controlled doors. All managed from a single front desk encoder station with real-time PMS integration.

🖨️ Encoder Systems

Front Desk Encoder

Connected to PMS at check-in desk. Encodes guest key cards for room number, check-in date/time, check-out date/time, and permitted common area access (pool, gym etc.).

  • PMS sends room assignment + dates to encoder
  • Encoder writes encrypted token to card
  • Duplicate key: re-encode same room assignment
  • Early check-in / late check-out: update timestamp on card
  • Room move: encode new room, old key automatically invalid

📱 Mobile Key Workflow

⚠ Blind Spot — Housekeeping Masters With No Expiry

The single most common hotel security vulnerability: housekeeping master keys programmed with no expiry date. A staff member who leaves the hotel takes a fully functional grandmaster or floor master with them. Every hotel master key must have a programmed expiry date — 6 months maximum — and must be re-encoded on every staff credential review cycle.

Stage 15 Key Takeaways

  • Hotel locks are predominantly offline — credentials stored locally on the lock, not requiring live server connection
  • Key hierarchy spans 6 levels from guest key to grandmaster — each level strictly scoped
  • MIFARE Classic is in millions of hotels and is broken crypto — recommend DESFire EV3 for upgrades
  • Mobile keys (BLE / NFC wallet) are the premium offering — highest guest satisfaction scores
  • Housekeeping masters without expiry are the #1 hotel security vulnerability — always set an expiry date
  • Online locks for BOH and premium areas; offline for standard guest rooms — hybrid strategy

The PMS Is the Heart of Hotel Operations

The Property Management System (PMS) is where reservations, room assignments, billing, housekeeping, and maintenance all converge. Your access control integration must be seamless with the PMS — a broken link between PMS and lock system means guests can't get into their rooms, and that's a crisis at any hour.

🏨 Major Hotel PMS Platforms

Enterprise

The dominant enterprise hotel PMS globally. Used by most 4- and 5-star chains. Deep API integration with all major lock vendors.

🔄 Full PMS ↔ Lock System Data Flow

🛎️ Check-In to Door — The Complete Workflow

  1. Reservation created in PMS: Room number, check-in date/time, check-out date/time, guest name, common area access (pool, gym, parking)
  2. Check-in triggered: Receptionist completes check-in in PMS — reservation becomes active, triggers API call to lock interface
  3. Key encoded: Lock interface instructs encoder to write credential to card (or sends mobile key to guest app)
  4. Key issued to guest: Card handed over or push notification sent — guest proceeds directly to room
  5. Room move or upgrade: PMS room change triggers new key encode — old key automatically invalid at next lock challenge
  6. Late check-out: PMS extends checkout time — credential validity extended, pushed to lock system
  7. Check-out: PMS closes reservation — credential expiry timestamp has already invalidated the key (or immediate invalidation in online lock systems)

🧹 Housekeeping Integration

Housekeeping App Workflow

  • Attendant logs into housekeeping app on tablet/phone with their section master card or PIN
  • App shows their assigned room list with current status (Vacant Dirty / Occupied / Do Not Disturb)
  • Attendant enters room → lock records staff entry event (online locks only)
  • Cleaning complete → attendant marks room as Vacant Clean in app
  • Room status feeds back to PMS — available for same-day sell
  • DND signal from lock (guest engaged DND switch) shows in app — attendant skips room

Maintenance Integration

  • Work order raised in PMS/maintenance system → time-limited maintenance credential issued
  • Maintenance credential valid only for specific room on specific date — not a master key
  • Engineering master: access to all mechanical rooms, BOH — highest privilege staff credential
  • Lift machine room, plant room: highest security BOH doors — online locks recommended
  • Guest room entry by maintenance: notification generated to front desk when occupied room is entered

⚖️ GDPR in Hotels

⚠ Hotel Lock Audit Data = Personal Data

Every lock event for a guest room ties an individual to a location and time — this is personal data under GDPR (EU) and equivalent legislation. Hotels must: retain audit logs for the minimum necessary period (typically 90 days); not share log data without guest consent; respond to Subject Access Requests within 30 days; delete audit data on request unless a legal hold applies (e.g., incident investigation). Agree data retention policy with the hotel operator before system commissioning.

⚠ Blind Spot — Supervisor Masters Given to All Staff "For Convenience"

The most common hotel security failure found in post-incident investigations: floor master or section master keys given to every housekeeping staff member because it's "easier than issuing individual keys." When a guest reports a theft, the audit trail shows 12 different people could have entered the room. No conviction possible. Prosecution fails. Individual section masters scoped to each attendant's assigned rooms only — this is non-negotiable.
