Video Signal Basics

HDMI Standards, EDID, HDCP & Signal Flow

Welcome to Matrix Video Switching!

Before we can understand matrix switchers, we must first understand the video signal itself. This module covers the fundamental concepts that explain why signals break — before you ever touch any hardware. Understanding these principles will make every troubleshooting decision you make later in this course much clearer.

The Video Signal Journey

🎯 Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, you will understand:

  • How HDMI transports video data and why bandwidth matters
  • What EDID is and how it negotiates resolution between devices
  • How HDCP protects content and why it sometimes fails
  • The complete signal flow from source to display

Understanding HDMI Standards

HDMI (High-Definition Multimedia Interface) is the dominant digital video connection in professional and consumer AV. Understanding its evolution is critical for proper system design.

📺 HDMI 1.4

Bandwidth: 10.2 Gbps

Max Resolution: 4K @ 30Hz

Released: 2009

Introduced 4K support at 30Hz, HDMI Ethernet Channel (HEC), and Audio Return Channel (ARC). Still found in many older commercial displays.

Limitation: Cannot support 4K @ 60Hz, which is standard for most modern content and presentations.

📺 HDMI 2.0

Bandwidth: 18 Gbps

Max Resolution: 4K @ 60Hz

Released: 2013

The workhorse standard for years. Supports 4K @ 60Hz with full color depth, HDR metadata, and improved audio support including up to 32 audio channels.

Common Use: Most commercial displays, video switchers, and matrix processors from 2015-2020.

📺 HDMI 2.1

Bandwidth: 48 Gbps

Max Resolution: 8K @ 60Hz / 4K @ 120Hz

Released: 2017

The current premium standard. Supports 8K resolution, 4K @ 120Hz for gaming, Dynamic HDR, Variable Refresh Rate (VRR), and Enhanced Audio Return Channel (eARC).

Key Point: HDMI 2.1 cables (Ultra High Speed) are required for 48Gbps. Standard Premium High Speed cables max out at 18Gbps.

Bandwidth Math: Why It Matters

Understanding bandwidth requirements helps you diagnose why signals fail and which equipment you need for specific installations.

📊 Bandwidth Calculation Formula

Bandwidth = Resolution × Refresh Rate × Color Depth × Compression

1
Resolution

Total pixels (e.g., 3840×2160 = 8,294,400 pixels)

2
Refresh Rate

Frames per second (24, 30, 60, 120 Hz)

3
Color Depth

Bits per pixel (8-bit = 256 colors per channel, 10-bit = 1024)

4
Color Sampling

4:4:4 (full), 4:2:2 (compressed), 4:2:0 (heavily compressed)

4K @ 60Hz - 4:4:4 (No Compression)

3840 × 2160 × 60 × 3 channels × 8 bits

= ~12 Gbps (raw)

With 8b/10b encoding overhead: ~15 Gbps

Requirement: HDMI 2.0 (18 Gbps) minimum
Problem: HDMI 1.4 cables/ports CANNOT support this

4K @ 60Hz - 4:2:0 (HDR)

3840 × 2160 × 60 × 2 channels × 10 bits

= ~10 Gbps (raw)

With overhead: ~12.5 Gbps

Requirement: HDMI 2.0 (18 Gbps) works comfortably
Warning: Chroma subsampling reduces color resolution

⚠️ The Bandwidth Bottleneck

When you connect a new 4K @ 60Hz source to an older HDMI 1.4 display or through an older matrix switcher, you may experience:

  • No signal - The devices cannot negotiate a common resolution
  • Resolution drops to 1080p - The source automatically downscales
  • Refresh rate drops to 30Hz - Smooth motion becomes choppy
  • Intermittent signal loss - Marginal compatibility causes instability

Solution: Always verify the HDMI version of ALL devices in the signal chain, including cables.

EDID: The Display's Digital Business Card

EDID (Extended Display Identification Data) is how displays communicate their capabilities to sources. Think of it as the display handing the source a business card that says exactly what it can show.

📇 What EDID Contains

📐 Native Resolution

The display's optimal resolution (e.g., 1920×1080, 3840×2160)

🔄 Supported Refresh Rates

24Hz, 30Hz, 60Hz, 120Hz, etc.

🎨 Color Capabilities

Color depth, color space (RGB, YCbCr), HDR support

🔊 Audio Capabilities

Supported audio formats (PCM, Dolby, DTS)

⏱️ Timing Data

Exact timing parameters for proper signal delivery

📋 Manufacturer Info

Brand name, model number, serial number

How EDID Handshake Works

🔌

1. Connection Established

Source and display are physically connected via HDMI. The display's EDID is stored in a 128-byte (or 256-byte for HDMI 2.0+) data structure in the display's EEPROM.

→
📡

2. Source Requests EDID

When powered on, the source device (laptop, media player) sends an EDID request through the DDC (Display Data Channel) line at pins 15-16 of the HDMI connector.

→
📋

3. Display Sends Capabilities

The display responds with its EDID block, listing all supported resolutions, refresh rates, color formats, and audio capabilities in a standardized format.

→
🖥️

4. Source Adapts Output

The source reads the EDID and selects the best mutually-supported resolution and format. The video output is configured to match the display's capabilities.

→
✅

5. Signal Transmitted

Video is transmitted using the agreed-upon format. If the source cannot match any display capability, no signal is sent (common with 4K sources on 1080p displays).

⚠️ EDID Problems in Matrix Systems

When you add a matrix switcher between source and display, EDID complications arise:

  • EDID from output must be fed back to all inputs - Otherwise sources don't know what resolution to output
  • Mismatched displays cause conflicts - One 4K display and one 1080p display on the same matrix output create EDID confusion
  • EDID management is essential - Professional matrix switchers have EDID management to copy, preload, or customize EDID data

HDCP: Digital Content Protection

HDCP (High-bandwidth Digital Content Protection) is Intel's encryption system that prevents unauthorized copying of digital content. It's why you sometimes see "HDCP Error" on your screen.

🔐 HDCP 1.4

Version: 1.4

Max Resolution: 4K @ 30Hz

Released: 2009

Required for HDMI 1.4 implementations. Obsolete for modern 4K @ 60Hz content.

🔐 HDCP 2.2

Version: 2.2

Max Resolution: 4K @ 60Hz

Released: 2015

The current standard for 4K UHD content. Required for Netflix 4K, Amazon Prime 4K, Ultra HD Blu-ray, and modern gaming consoles. Not backwards compatible with HDCP 1.4.

🔐 HDCP 2.3

Version: 2.3

Features: Enhanced security

Released: 2018

Latest version with improved security features for 4K HDR content. Required for some newer streaming services and devices.

HDCP Authentication Process

🔑

1. Key Exchange

Source and display exchange their Key Selection Vectors (KSVs). Each device has a unique private key embedded by the manufacturer.

→
🔍

2. Authentication Verification

Using Blom's scheme, each device verifies the other has legitimate HDCP keys. Invalid or counterfeit keys cause immediate failure.

→
📍

3. Locality Check

The protocol enforces a 20ms round-trip time limit to ensure source and sink are physically close (prevents long-distance signal interception).

→
🔒

4. Session Key Exchange

A unique session key is generated for the connection. All video data is encrypted using this key before transmission.

→
📺

5. Continuous Verification

Throughout playback, devices continually verify the connection remains secure. Any tampering or unauthorized devices cause the signal to blank.

⚠️ HDCP Failure Scenarios

When HDCP fails, you typically see a blank screen or error message. Common causes include:

🔌 Non-HDCP Device in Chain

Any device (cable, adapter, switch, extender) that isn't HDCP-compliant will break the chain

📺 HDCP Version Mismatch

HDCP 2.2 source connected to HDCP 1.4-only display = no signal

🔄 EDID Stripping

Some extenders/switchers strip EDID, causing HDCP authentication to fail

🔀 Multiple Display Conflict

HDCP authorization with multiple displays can fail if they report different capabilities

Complete Signal Flow: Source to Display

Understanding the entire signal journey helps you identify where problems occur.

Common Signal Flow Problems

Problem Root Cause Solution
No Signal HDCP version mismatch or cable too long Verify all devices support same HDCP version
Wrong Resolution EDID mismatch or scaler disabled Use EDID management on matrix switcher
Intermittent Dropout Marginal cable quality or length limit exceeded Use active cable or fiber extender
Color Issues 4:4:4 not supported through system Ensure HDMI 2.0+ throughout chain
Audio Missing Audio not routed or ARC issue Verify audio breakout or ARC configuration

📝 Module 1 Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • HDMI Versions Matter: HDMI 1.4 (10.2 Gbps), HDMI 2.0 (18 Gbps), HDMI 2.1 (48 Gbps) - always verify versions in your signal chain
  • Bandwidth Math: Resolution × Refresh Rate × Color Depth determines required bandwidth
  • EDID is Handshake: Displays tell sources their capabilities via EDID; sources adapt their output accordingly
  • HDCP Protects Content: Encryption requires all devices in chain to be HDCP compliant with matching versions
  • Know Your Limits: Copper HDMI cables max out around 10-15 feet for 4K @ 60Hz; use extenders for longer runs
Module 2

Point to Point and Extension

Direct Connections, Extenders, Distance Limitations & Latency

Understanding Signal Extension

In an ideal world, every source would connect directly to its display with a simple cable. In the real world, sources and displays are often separated by significant distances — sometimes hundreds of feet. This module covers the technologies that bridge those gaps and the trade-offs you need to understand when choosing an extension solution.

Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, you will understand:

  • Why copper HDMI cables have strict length limitations
  • How HDBaseT extenders work and their 100m capability
  • When to use fiber optic extenders for long distances
  • Latency considerations for different extension technologies
  • How to properly install and troubleshoot extenders

The Direct HDMI Connection

A direct HDMI connection is the simplest solution when the source and display are within cable length limitations.

⚠️ Copper HDMI Cable Limitations

Standard copper HDMI cables have strict distance limitations that decrease as bandwidth increases:

HDMI 1.4 (10.2 Gbps)

1080p @ 60Hz: 10-15 meters (33-50 ft)

4K @ 30Hz: 5-8 meters (16-26 ft)

HDMI 2.0 (18 Gbps)

1080p @ 60Hz: 7-10 meters (23-33 ft)

4K @ 60Hz: 3-5 meters (10-16 ft)

HDMI 2.1 (48 Gbps)

4K @ 60Hz: 2-3 meters (6-10 ft)

8K @ 60Hz: 1-2 meters (3-6 ft)

Why the limitation? At high data rates (18-48 Gbps), signal integrity degrades over copper due to attenuation (signal loss), electromagnetic interference (EMI), and skin effect at high frequencies.

Types of HDMI Cables

Standard HDMI Cable (Passive)

  • Length Limit: 3-15 meters depending on bandwidth
  • Cost: Inexpensive ($10-30)
  • Best For: Short runs, home theater, desktop setups
  • Limitation: No signal regeneration
Tip: Look for "Premium High Speed" certification for HDMI 2.0 compliance.

Active HDMI Cable

  • Length Limit: Up to 25-30 meters (82-100 ft)
  • Cost: Moderate ($50-100)
  • Best For: Medium runs, cleaner installation
  • Feature: Built-in signal amplification
Caution: Directional! The transmitter end must connect to the source.

HDBaseT: The Industry Standard Extender

HDBaseT is the dominant technology for extending HDMI signals over standard network cable. Developed by the HDBaseT Alliance (now part of the Valens semiconductor company), it transmits uncompressed video, audio, power, controls, and Ethernet over a single CAT cable.

📡 HDBaseT 2.0

Bandwidth: 10.2 Gbps

Max Distance: 100m (328 ft) over CAT5e/6

Max Resolution: 4K @ 30Hz

Released: 2012

The original HDBaseT standard, still widely used. Supports 5Play feature set: video, audio, controls, Ethernet, and power.

📡 HDBaseT 3.0

Bandwidth: 18 Gbps (uncompressed)

Max Distance: 100m (328 ft) over CAT6a

Max Resolution: 4K @ 60Hz 4:4:4

Released: 2019

The current standard. Supports full 18Gbps HDMI 2.0 with zero latency, better immunity to cable quality, and enhanced 5Play features.

The 5Play Feature Set

HDBaseT's power comes from bundling multiple signals over a single cable:

📺

Uncompressed Video

Full-bandwidth HDMI up to 4K @ 60Hz with zero latency

🔊

Audio

All HDMI audio formats including Dolby Atmos, DTS:X

🎮

Controls

IR, RS-232, and CEC pass-through for device control

🌐

Ethernet

100Mbps Ethernet pass-through for network access

⚡

Power (PoC)

Up to 100W power over cable - receiver can be powered from transmitter

⚠️ HDBaseT Requirements

To achieve maximum distance and performance with HDBaseT:

  • CAT6a cable recommended for HDBaseT 3.0 (CAT5e/6 works for shorter runs)
  • Solid copper conductors - CCA (copper-clad aluminum) will cause failures
  • Proper termination - use 8P8C connectors with correct pinout (T568A or T568B)
  • No patch panels in middle - direct cable run preferred

Fiber Optic Extenders

For distances beyond 100m, or in environments with high electromagnetic interference, fiber optic extenders are the solution. Fiber transmits light rather than electrical signals, eliminating EMI concerns and enabling transmission over many kilometers.

Multi-Mode Fiber (OM3/OM4)

Distance: 300-400 meters (984-1312 ft)

Bandwidth: Up to 18 Gbps

Cost: Lower per meter than single-mode

  • Uses 850nm wavelength (typically)
  • Higher modal dispersion limits distance
  • Less expensive optics and cable
  • Best For: Campus buildings, moderate distances

Single-Mode Fiber (OS1/OS2)

Distance: 10+ km (6+ miles) - unlimited for AV

Bandwidth: Up to 48+ Gbps

Cost: Higher per meter but lower long-term

  • Uses 1310nm or 1550nm wavelength
  • No modal dispersion - virtually unlimited distance
  • More expensive optics required
  • Best For: Stadiums, campuses, long-haul
🔬 SFP+ Modules: Most fiber extenders use pluggable SFP+ (Small Form-factor Pluggable) transceivers. Common types include:
  • Multi-mode SFP+ (850nm): For OM3/OM4 fiber, up to 300-400m
  • Single-mode SFP+ (1310nm): For OS1/OS2 fiber, up to 10km+
  • Bi-directional (BiDi): Uses single fiber strand (Tx/Rx on different wavelengths)

Distance Comparison Chart

Technology Max Distance 4K @ 60Hz Best Use Case

Understanding Video Latency

Latency is the time delay between a source generating a signal and it appearing on the display. For most applications, latency below 50ms is imperceptible. For interactive applications (gaming, video conferencing), lower latency is critical.

⚡ HDBaseT

Latency: Near zero (~0ms)

Compression: None

HDBaseT transmits uncompressed video, resulting in virtually zero added latency. This makes it ideal for live video, gaming, and interactive applications.

⚡ SDVoE / IP Video

Latency: 1-5ms (typical)

Compression: JPEG 2000 or none

SDVoE using uncompressed or lightly compressed (JPEG 2000) video maintains very low latency. Perfect for latency-critical applications when properly configured.

⚡ H.265/HEVC Extenders

Latency: 30-100ms+

Compression: Heavy (H.265)

Compression-based extenders introduce significant latency due to encoding/decoding delay. May cause lip-sync issues and is unsuitable for real-time interaction.

Installation Best Practices

📏

1. Measure Distance

Measure the actual cable run distance, not straight-line distance. Add 10-15% for slack and termination.

→
🔌

2. Choose Technology

Match extender technology to distance and latency requirements. Don't over-spec or under-spec.

→
🔍

3. Verify Cable Quality

Test cable continuity and quality before installation. CCA cable will cause HDBaseT failures.

→
🔧

4. Terminate Properly

Use proper termination techniques. For HDBaseT, use 8P8C connectors with T568A/B pinout.

→
✅

5. Test Before Mounting

Verify end-to-end signal before final installation. It's much harder to troubleshoot after mounting.

📝 Module 2 Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Copper HDMI Limits: Passive cables max out at 3-5m for 4K @ 60Hz; active cables extend to 25-30m
  • HDBaseT Standard: Extends HDMI 100m over CAT cable with zero latency and 5Play features
  • HDBaseT 3.0 Required: For 4K @ 60Hz 4:4:4 - HDBaseT 2.0 only supports 4K @ 30Hz
  • Fiber for Distance: Multi-mode: 300-400m, Single-mode: 10km+ with no EMI concerns
  • Latency Matters: HDBaseT (~0ms) vs compression-based (30-100ms+)

Module 3

Distribution and Switching

Splitters, Distribution Amps, Video Switchers & Meeting Room Use Cases

Expanding Beyond Point-to-Point

Once you understand how to get a signal from point A to point B, the next challenge is distributing that signal to multiple destinations or switching between multiple sources. This module covers the fundamental technologies for one-to-many and many-to-one signal routing, which form the backbone of most commercial AV installations.

Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, you will understand:

  • The difference between splitters and distribution amplifiers
  • When to use a simple switcher vs. a matrix switcher
  • Common meeting room configurations and their requirements
  • How to properly wire and test distribution systems
  • Troubleshooting techniques for multi-output systems

One to Many: Distribution Fundamentals

The simplest expansion beyond point-to-point is duplicating a single source to multiple displays. This is where splitters and distribution amplifiers come into play.

🔀 HDMI Splitter

Function: Duplicate 1 source to N displays

Typical Sizes: 1x2, 1x4, 1x8, 1x16

Signal: Identical output on all ports

A passive signal splitter divides the electrical signal. All outputs show exactly the same content at the same time.

Use Cases: Digital signage, menu boards, meeting room displays showing same content

📢 Distribution Amplifier (DA)

Function: Buffer + amplify 1 source to N displays

Typical Sizes: 1x2, 1x4, 1x8, 1x16

Signal: Full-strength on all outputs

An active device with signal buffering. Each output has its own amplification, preventing signal degradation when driving multiple displays.

Use Cases: Critical installations, long cable runs, when signal integrity is paramount

⚠️ Key Difference: Passive vs Active

While the terms "splitter" and "distribution amplifier" are sometimes used interchangeably, the distinction is crucial:

Passive Splitter

  • Simple electrical division
  • Signal strength splits (halved for 1x2)
  • Lower cost
  • Limited to 2-3 displays
  • Short cable runs only
  • No power required

Distribution Amplifier

  • Active signal regeneration
  • Full-strength on each output
  • Higher cost
  • Supports 4+ displays easily
  • Longer cable runs supported
  • Power required (AC adapter)

HDCP Considerations for Distribution

Distributing HDCP-protected content requires special attention. Not all splitters support HDCP passthrough correctly.

🔐 HDCP Compliance in Distribution

When distributing protected content (Netflix, Blu-ray, etc.):

  • All displays must be HDCP compliant
  • The splitter/DA must support HDCP passthrough
  • EDID must be properly managed (often by "learning" from displays)
  • HDCP 2.2 required for 4K content

Many to One: Input Switching

When you have multiple sources but only one display (or one destination), you need a switcher to select which source to show.

🔘 Manual Switcher

Control: Push button on device

Inputs: 2-4 typical

Cost: $30-150

Simple button-press switching. No intelligence, no auto-detection. Best for: Home theater, simple meeting rooms.

🤖 Auto-Switcher

Control: Auto-detects active input

Inputs: 2-8 typical

Cost: $100-500

Automatically switches to the most recently activated source. No user intervention required. Best for: Conference rooms, huddle spaces.

🎛️ Presentation Switcher

Control: Auto + manual + control system

Inputs: 4-8+ (HDMI, VGA, USB-C)

Cost: $500-2000+

Multi-format inputs, scaling, audio embedding, and control system integration. Best for: Corporate conference rooms, training rooms.

Meeting Room Use Cases

Different meeting room sizes and functions require different AV configurations:

🗄️

Huddle Space (2-4 people)

Sources: 1-2 (laptop, phone)

Displays: 1 (TV or monitor)

Solution: Simple auto-switcher

Example: Small meeting room with table box, 2 HDMI inputs, single display
🏢

Conference Room (6-12 people)

Sources: 3-4 (laptops, document camera)

Displays: 1-2 (main display, confidence monitor)

Solution: Presentation switcher with scaling

Example: Boardroom with presentation switcher, main display + confidence monitor, audio system
🎓

Training Room (10-30 people)

Sources: 4-6 (multiple presenters)

Displays: 2-3 (main + auxiliary)

Solution: Matrix switcher or larger presentation switcher

Example: Training room with instructor station, multiple displays for visibility
🎭

Boardroom (10-20 people)

Sources: 4-8 (laptops, video conferencing)

Displays: 2-4 (main, side, confidence)

Solution: Full matrix switcher with video conferencing integration

Example: Executive boardroom with dual displays, video codec, audio conferencing

Video Switcher vs Matrix Switcher

A common point of confusion is the difference between a video switcher and a matrix switcher. The key difference is flexibility:

Video Switcher (Many-to-One)

  • 3 inputs → 1 output
  • Only ONE source visible at a time
  • Simple selection control
  • Fixed routing: Any input → Single output
  • Lower cost
Analogy: A single-pole, multi-throw switch - only one path active at a time

Matrix Switcher (Many-to-Many)

  • 3 inputs → 3 outputs (independent)
  • Multiple sources visible simultaneously
  • Independent routing per output
  • Flexible routing: Any input → Any output (simultaneously)
  • Higher cost
Analogy: A telephone exchange - any input can connect to any output independently

Module 4

True Matrix Switching

Matrix Architecture, Routing Logic & Audio Breakaway

The Power of Any-to-Any Routing

In previous modules, we explored one-to-many distribution (splitters/DAs) and many-to-one switching (video switchers). Now we reach the most flexible solution: true matrix switching. With a matrix, you can route any input to any output - independently and simultaneously. This is the foundation of professional AV systems where multiple sources need to reach multiple destinations flexibly.

Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, you will understand:

  • How matrix switchers are architected (crosspoint architecture)
  • The difference between blocking and non-blocking matrices
  • How audio breakaway routing works
  • EDID management strategies in matrix systems
  • Control methods: front panel, IR, RS-232, IP, API
  • How to design and test complex routing scenarios

Matrix Architecture: The Crosspoint

A matrix switcher gets its name from the mathematical concept of a matrix - a grid where every input can connect to every output. The core technology is the crosspoint switch, an array of switches at each input-output intersection.

📐 Matrix Size Notation

8x8 Matrix: 8 inputs, 8 outputs

16x16 Matrix: 16 inputs, 16 outputs

32x32 Matrix: 32 inputs, 32 outputs

The notation "AxB" means "A inputs, B outputs". An 8x4 matrix has 8 inputs but only 4 outputs - outputs are the limiting factor.

Example: 16x8 matrix can route any of 16 sources to any of 8 displays simultaneously

🔄 Non-Blocking Architecture

Definition: All inputs can reach all outputs simultaneously

Requirement: Full crosspoint switch fabric

Implication: N² crosspoints for NxN matrix

A non-blocking matrix can connect every input to every output at the same time, with no restriction on combinations.

Math: An 8x8 non-blocking matrix needs 64 (8×8) independent crosspoints

⚠️ Blocking vs Non-Blocking

Understanding blocking behavior is crucial for system design:

Non-Blocking Matrix

  • Full crosspoint switch fabric
  • All inputs can reach all outputs simultaneously
  • Simultaneous routing: IN1→OUT1, IN2→OUT2, etc.
  • Higher cost (more crosspoints)
  • Required for: Mission-critical applications

Blocking Matrix

  • Limited switch fabric
  • Cannot route all combinations simultaneously
  • Once output is used, input may be "blocked"
  • Lower cost
  • Warning: Check specifications carefully!
Always Verify: Professional-grade matrix switchers are typically non-blocking. Budget models may have blocking limitations.

Audio Breakaway: Independent Audio Routing

One of the most powerful features of professional matrix switchers is audio breakaway - the ability to route video and audio independently. This means the audio from Input 1 can go to Output 3 while Video 1 goes to Output 1.

Resolution Management

The Resolution Mismatch Challenge

In an ideal world, all sources would output the same resolution as all displays. In reality, you'll have 4K sources feeding 1080p displays, 1080p sources driving 4K screens, and everything in between. This module covers how to manage resolution mismatches through scaling and smart EDID management.

Learning Outcomes

  • When to upscale vs. downscale (and the trade-offs)
  • Aspect ratio fundamentals (4:3, 16:9, 21:9)

  • EDID strategy for mixed-resolution environments
  • How to configure scaling in matrix switchers

What is Video Scaling?

A video scaler converts video from one resolution to another, adjusting pixel count to match the target display while attempting to maintain image quality.

📈 Upscaling

Process: Low resolution → High resolution

Example: 1080p → 4K

Pixel Count: 2.25x increase

The scaler interpolates new pixels based on surrounding pixels. Modern scalers use sophisticated algorithms to smooth edges.

Limitation: Upscaling cannot create detail that wasn't in the original.

📉 Downscaling

Process: High resolution → Low resolution

Example: 4K → 1080p

Pixel Count: 4x reduction

The scaler combines or averages pixels to fit the smaller frame. This is higher quality than upscaling.

Best Practice: Always downscale when possible - mathematically cleaner, preserves detail.

Aspect Ratio Fundamentals

Aspect ratio is the relationship between width and height (W:H). Different formats use different ratios:

📦 Letterbox

Use when: Wider content (2.35:1) on narrower display (16:9)

Result: Black bars added top and bottom

📐 Pillarbox

Use when: Narrower content (4:3) on wider display (16:9)

Result: Black bars added left and right

EDID Strategy for Mixed Resolution

📋 EDID Strategy Options
Highest Common Denominator:

Set all inputs to 1080p. All displays work but 4K displays are underutilized.

Per-Output Scaling:

Use matrix with per-output scalers. Each output scales to match its display.

4K Matrix with Downscaling:

Some matrices output 4K to one output and 1080p to another from same input.

Common Resolution Combinations

Source Display Action Quality
4K 4K Pass-through ✅ Best
4K 1080p Downscale ✅ Excellent
1080p 4K Upscale ⚠️ Good
1080p 1080p Pass-through ✅ Best

📝 Module 5 Summary

Key Takeaways:

  • Upscaling: Adds pixels via interpolation - cannot create detail, may soften image
  • Downscaling: Removes pixels via averaging - always higher quality
  • Aspect Ratios: 4:3 (1.33:1), 16:9 (1.78:1), 21:9 (2.39:1)
  • Letterbox: Bars top/bottom for wider content; Pillarbox: bars left/right for narrower content
  • Mixed Resolution: Use per-output scaling or downscaling matrices
  • EDID Strategy: Set EDID to match display capabilities, not source capabilities

The Evolution of Video Distribution

Video Over IP (AV over IP) replaces traditional matrix switches with network-based distribution. Video is encoded, sent over the network, and decoded at the display. This approach offers unlimited scalability, lower cabling costs, and integration with existing IT infrastructure.

Learning Outcomes

After completing this module, you will understand:

  • How encoder/decoder systems work in AV over IP
  • The difference between compressed and uncompressed video
  • Network switch bandwidth requirements and calculations
  • Major manufacturers: Crestron NVX, SDVoE, Barco, ZeeVee

  • Network design best practices

How Video Over IP Works

AV over IP converts video signals into network data packets that travel over standard Ethernet infrastructure.

📦 Encoder

Input: HDMI, DisplayPort

Output: IP stream over Ethernet

Function: Compresses video for network

Located at the video source end. Converts baseband video into network-transportable data.

📡 Decoder

Input: IP stream over Ethernet

Output: HDMI

Function: Decompresses for display

Located at the display end. Converts network data back to baseband video for screens.

🌐 Network Switch

Input: Multiple IP streams

Function: Routes video between encoders/decoders

The backbone that moves video data across the network. Must meet AV bandwidth requirements.

Compressed vs Uncompressed Video

The choice between compressed and uncompressed video impacts bandwidth requirements, latency, and image quality.

Feature Uncompressed Compressed
Bandwidth (4K60 4:4:4) 18 Gbps 20-50 Mbps
Network Required 10GbE minimum Standard 1GbE
Latency < 1ms 16-32ms typical

⚠️ Latency Matters

Real-time video applications require low latency:

  • Interactive displays: Need < 50ms latency to feel responsive
  • Video conferencing: Lip sync issues above 100ms
  • Gaming/live production: Need < 16ms (one frame) for real-time

Compressed AV over IP typically adds 16-32ms of latency - acceptable for most applications but unsuitable for real-time production.

Bandwidth Calculations for AV over IP

Use the AVIXA bandwidth formula to calculate network requirements:

📐 Total Video Bandwidth Equation

Bandwidth = HP × VP × FR × BD × 3 × CDF × 1.25

Where:

  • HP = Horizontal Pixels
  • VP = Vertical Pixels
  • FR = Frame Rate (fps)
  • BD = Bit Depth (bits per channel, usually 8 or 10)
  • 3 = RGB channels (red, green, blue)
  • CDF = Chroma Decimation Factor (1.0 for 4:4:4, 0.5 for 4:2:2, 0.25 for 4:2:0)
  • 1.25 = Overhead factor for DVI/HDMI protocols

🔢 Example Calculations

1
4K60 4:4:4 Uncompressed

3840 × 2160 × 60 × 10 × 3 × 1.0 × 1.25 = 18.0 Gbps

2
4K60 4:2:2 Compressed (typical)

3840 × 2160 × 60 × 10 × 3 × 0.5 × 1.25 = 9.0 Gbps raw

After compression: ~20-50 Mbps depending on codec efficiency

3
1080p60 4:4:4 Uncompressed

1920 × 1080 × 60 × 10 × 3 × 1.0 × 1.25 = 4.5 Gbps

Multicast Fundamentals

Multicast is essential for efficient AV over IP distribution. Unlike unicast (one-to-one), multicast allows one source to send to many destinations efficiently.

📡 Multicast Addresses

Range: 224.0.0.0 to 239.255.255.255

AV Use: Typically 239.x.x.x

Each video stream uses a unique multicast address. Encoders transmit to this address; decoders subscribe to receive it.

🔄 IGMP Snooping

Purpose: Optimize multicast traffic

Benefit: Switches only send streams to requested ports

Critical for AV over IP. Without IGMP snooping, multicast floods all ports, overwhelming the network.

🏷️ Multicast VLAN (MVR)

Purpose: Isolate multicast traffic

Benefit: Separate from data VLAN

Creates a dedicated VLAN for all multicast video traffic, improving security and management.

Video Scaling in AV Systems

Scaling adjusts video resolution to match the display's native resolution. Proper scaling is critical for image quality.

⬆️ Upscaling

Input: Lower resolution (e.g., 1080p)

Output: Higher resolution (e.g., 4K)

Adding pixels to fill a larger display. Good scalers use intelligent interpolation for smooth results.

Best Practice: Upscale at the display or processor, not at source

⬇️ Downscaling

Input: Higher resolution (e.g., 4K)

Output: Lower resolution (e.g., 1080p)

Removing pixels to fit a smaller display. Essential for video walls with non-native sources.

Best Practice: Use high-quality hardware scalers for critical content

🔄 Native Resolution

Ideal: Match source to display native resolution

Benefit: No scaling artifacts

Best quality when source resolution matches display native resolution. Plan sources accordingly.

Best Practice: EDID management to negotiate optimal resolution

⚠️ Scaling Artifacts

Poor scaling creates visible problems:

  • Aliasing: Jagged edges on diagonal lines
  • Blockiness: Loss of fine detail
  • Softening: Loss of image sharpness
  • Frame rate issues: Judder during frame rate conversion

Solution: Use professional-grade scalers with high-quality processing algorithms.

Scaling Best Practices

📐 Scaling Guidelines
1. Scale Once: Only scale video once in the signal chain. Multiple scaling degrades quality.
2. Scale at Display: Let displays handle their own scaling when possible.
3. Match Aspect Ratios: Maintain source aspect ratio to prevent stretching.
4. Test Critical Content: Verify text and graphics legibility after scaling.

Testing & Commissioning Procedures

Systematic testing ensures everything works correctly before client handover.

✅ Commissioning Checklist

1
Pre-Power Check
  • All cables securely connected
  • No visible cable damage
  • Ground loops verified
  • Rack ventilation adequate
2
Power-Up Sequence
  • Turn on UPS first
  • Power network equipment
  • Power displays and sources
  • Power amplifiers LAST
  • Verify no fault indicators
3
Signal Path Testing
  • Test every source to every display
  • Verify resolution matches source
  • Test audio routing
  • Verify HDCP passthrough
  • Test control system functions
4
Burn-In Period
  • Run system 4-8 hours continuously
  • Monitor for overheating
  • Check audio quality at volume
  • Verify reliability
5
Client Training
  • Demonstrate all functions
  • Provide quick-reference guide
  • Explain troubleshooting steps
  • Schedule follow-up support
